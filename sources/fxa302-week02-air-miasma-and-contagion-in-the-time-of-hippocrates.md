## Air, Miasma and Contagion in the Time of Hippocrates

This chapter explores the concept of miasma in ancient Greece, particularly its role in understanding disease and contagion during the time of Hippocrates. Miasma, a term derived from 'to stain', initially pertained to religious and legal contexts but later found usage in medical texts related to disease. The author examines how miasma was used in both religious medicine, where it was linked with contamination and purifications for diseases like epilepsy, and rational medicine, which associated miasma with the air quality and its influence on health. The chapter highlights the persistence of the term miasma after Hippocrates but notes that it did not carry forward the complete theory. This exploration underscores the historical evolution of medical thought in ancient Greece concerning disease transmission and environmental impacts on human health.
