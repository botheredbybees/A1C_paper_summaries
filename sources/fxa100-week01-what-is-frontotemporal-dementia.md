## What is Frontotemporal Dementia?

Frontotemporal dementia (FTD) refers to a group of conditions characterized by damage to nerve cells in the frontal and temporal lobes of the brain, which control behavior, emotional responses, and language. FTD typically affects younger individuals under 65 years old more frequently than other forms of dementia. The most common type of FTD involves early damage to the frontal lobe leading to changes in personality and loss of inhibitions, while another type causes difficulties with language due to temporal lobe damage. These conditions are thought to arise from abnormal protein accumulation that leads to nerve cell death. Unlike many other dementias, there is often a strong genetic link associated with FTD. The progression rate varies, but symptoms tend to overlap over time. Behavioral changes can be challenging for both the affected individual and caregivers; however, engaging in meaningful activities can mitigate some behaviors. Non-verbal communication methods or shorter sentences are recommended when dealing with language difficulties.
