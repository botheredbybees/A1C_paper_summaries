## Cognitive Reserve in Healthy Aging and Alzheimer’s Disease: A Meta-Analysis of fMRI Studies

The study examines cognitive reserve (CR) in both healthy aging and Alzheimer’s disease (AD) through a meta-analysis of functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) studies. Cognitive reserve is defined as the ability to optimize performance by recruiting alternative neural networks, which may mitigate age-related cognitive decline or symptoms of neurodegenerative diseases like AD. The research synthesizes data from 17 fMRI studies and identifies distinct patterns of brain activation associated with CR proxies in healthy elderly individuals and those with AD or mild cognitive impairment (MCI). In healthy aging, a wide network including the medial and lateral frontal areas was found to be active. For patients with AD and amnesic-MCI, activations were more localized to the anterior cingulate cortex. The findings suggest potential compensatory mechanisms in response to brain damage or age-related changes, contributing to understanding how neural networks support cognitive resilience.
