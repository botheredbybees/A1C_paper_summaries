## Eudaimonic well-being: Early humanistic approaches - Abraham Maslow and self-actualisation

This document outlines the concept of eudaimonic well-being through the lens of Abraham Maslow's early humanistic approach, focusing on self-actualization. It begins by discussing Maslow’s hierarchy of needs, distinguishing between deficiency (D) and being or 'growth' needs (B). The hierarchy emphasizes that as individuals meet their basic physiological and safety needs, they move towards fulfilling higher-level psychological needs such as love/belonging, esteem, and eventually self-actualization. Self-transcendence is also mentioned as a further stage beyond self-actualization.

The document details the characteristics of self-actualized individuals, including efficient perception of reality, acceptance, continued freshness of appreciation, spontaneity, creativity, peak experiences, autonomy, detachment, resistance to enculturation, social interest (Gemeinschaftsgefuhl), deep interpersonal relations, philosophical sense of humor, problem-centering, democratic character structure, and discrimination between means and ends. It is noted that only a small percentage of the population reaches self-actualization, based on detailed case studies of high achievers.

This content is relevant to the creative arts and health fields by providing foundational knowledge about humanistic psychology's perspective on well-being, highlighting the importance of personal growth and fulfillment in achieving optimal mental health.
