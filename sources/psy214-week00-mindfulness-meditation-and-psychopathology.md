## Mindfulness Meditation and Psychopathology

This review article examines the current understanding of mindfulness meditation (MM) through a clinical neuroscience lens, focusing on its applications in mental health interventions and basic research on psychopathology. The authors outline core capacities targeted by MM practices such as reducing distraction and enhancing awareness of mental experiences. They map these capacities onto cognitive and affective constructs within the Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) framework proposed by the National Institute of Mental Health, emphasizing how mindfulness can address transdiagnostic factors in a variety of psychopathological conditions including depression, anxiety, chronic pain, and substance abuse.

The review highlights efficacious applications of MM to specific domains of mental health disorders and discusses emerging efforts for attention disorders, traumatic stress, disordered eating, obesity, and serious mental illness. The authors also address practical concerns related to implementation, dissemination, training fidelity, and cultural fit while acknowledging methodological challenges such as the risk of hype and potential adverse events.

The central argument underscores MM's versatility in treating a wide range of conditions and its growing importance in clinical practice despite ongoing need for rigorous research methodologies. This work is highly relevant to creative arts and health fields by providing empirical evidence that supports the integration of mindfulness practices into therapeutic interventions, contributing to resilience and overall well-being.
