## Forms of Puppets and Puppetry

The document 'Forms of Puppets and Puppetry' explores the historical development and cultural significance of puppetry across various civilizations, including ancient times in Europe, China, Korea, Japan, and Greece. It begins with an archaeological discovery of a mammoth tusk figure dating back to around 26,000 years ago found near Brno in the Czech Republic. The document also mentions intricate Egyptian mechanized figures used for entertainment purposes and elaborates on the use of puppets during the Greek civilization, where they were employed both as part of religious processions and theatrical productions. It highlights the literary references to puppets by Ancient Greek philosophers like Plato.

Moving eastward, it discusses Chinese shadow puppets made from thin leather painted with bright colors that appeared as colored figures on a translucent screen. Puppetry in China, Korea, Japan, and South Asia was an integral part of storytelling and cultural festivals. The document introduces Bunraku puppet theatre from Japan, a form dating back to around 1600 which uses large puppets manipulated by multiple puppeteers.

The text also covers European puppet traditions influenced by Greek and Roman practices, with records showing the use of puppets in religious processions during the Middle Ages. It details the evolution of Commedia dell'arte, an improvisational theatrical form popularized in Italy and adapted easily for puppet performances. The document further explores the emergence of 'Opera dei Pupi,' a Sicilian genre featuring detailed marionettes depicting medieval knights, as well as British puppet shows, particularly those of Martin Powell.
