## “Telling me not to worry…” Hyperscanning and Neural Dynamics of Emotion Processing During Guided Imagery and Music

This paper explores the neural dynamics of emotion processing during Guided Imagery and Music (GIM) sessions, a therapeutic technique that involves clients experiencing spontaneous imagery while listening to music under the guidance of a therapist. The study employs dual-EEG technology to measure brain activity in both the therapist (“Guide”) and client (“Traveler”). It focuses on pivotal moments of interest within these sessions where significant emotional and cognitive processing occurs. By analyzing indices such as frontal and parietal alpha asymmetry, frontal midline theta, and occipital alpha activity, researchers found that the emotions experienced by both participants were not positive or relaxed but rather involved dealing with negative feelings which had to be managed through interpersonal processes. The study highlights temporal shifts in emotional markers during critical moments of interaction, indicating a mutual recognition of emotional states between therapist and client. This research underscores the importance of understanding brain mechanisms in therapeutic interactions, contributing valuable insights into how music therapy works on a neurological level.
