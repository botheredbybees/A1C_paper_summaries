## Concepts related to hedonia or ‘happiness’

The document explores concepts related to hedonia, or happiness, focusing on affective forecasting and the hedonic treadmill theories. Affective forecasting refers to our ability to predict future emotions based on current life events, which research suggests we are often inaccurate at predicting both positive and negative impacts of such events (Brickman et al., 1978). This study highlights that individuals tend to overestimate how intensely they will be affected by life changes and underestimate their resilience in adapting. The hedonic treadmill theory posits a constant cycle where people strive for goals, achieve them, feel temporarily elated but then adapt back to baseline levels of happiness (Lykken & Tallegen, 1996; Diener et al., 2006). Additionally, the document discusses how wealth and materialism influence well-being, suggesting that hedonic adaptation can diminish appreciation for possessions over time (Kahneman & Deaton, 2010; Kahneman, 2011; Donelley et al., 2018), implying a cycle where more is never enough. This highlights the importance of understanding these psychological concepts in fostering sustainable happiness and self-care practices.
