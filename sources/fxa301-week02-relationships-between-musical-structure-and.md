## Relationships Between Musical Structure and Psychophysiological Measures of Emotion

This study explores the relationship between musical structure and psychophysiological measures of emotion, focusing on experienced pleasantness and arousal rather than perceived emotions. The authors investigate how various structural features of music (such as mode, harmonic complexity, tempo, accentuation, and rhythmic articulation) influence both subjective reports and physiological responses like respiration, skin conductance, and heart rate. Key findings indicate that specific musical elements correlate with emotional experiences: mode, harmonic complexity, and rhythmic articulation distinguish between negative and positive valence; while tempo, accentuation, and rhythmic articulation differentiate high arousal from low arousal. Faster breathing, increased skin conductance, and higher heart rates were observed in music characterized by faster tempos, accentuation, and staccato rhythms. The study suggests that rhythmic aspects of music are crucial for inducing physiological responses. This research is relevant to the creative arts and health fields as it enhances understanding of how music affects emotional and physiological states.
