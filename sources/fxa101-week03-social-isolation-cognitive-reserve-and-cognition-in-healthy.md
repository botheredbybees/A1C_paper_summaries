## Social isolation, cognitive reserve, and cognition in healthy older people

This research article explores the relationship between social isolation and cognitive function in healthy older adults, considering the role of cognitive reserve as a moderating factor. The study utilizes data from the Cognitive Function and Ageing Study–Wales (CFAS-Wales) to analyze baseline and two-year follow-up information on social isolation, cognitive function, and cognitive reserve. Social isolation is assessed using the Lubben Social Network Scale-6 (LSNS-6), cognitive function is evaluated with the Cambridge Cognitive Examination (CAMCOG), and cognitive reserve is gauged through measures such as education level, occupational complexity, and cognitive activity engagement. The findings reveal that social isolation negatively correlates with cognitive function at both baseline and follow-up stages after controlling for demographic factors and health conditions. Furthermore, cognitive reserve mitigates this relationship over time, suggesting that sustained social engagement can enhance cognitive reserve and improve cognitive outcomes in later life. This study has implications for interventions aimed at reducing social isolation to maintain cognitive health.
