## Arts in Dementia Care: This is not the end... if it's the end of this chapter

Anne Davis Basting discusses the role of arts and creative expression for individuals with dementia. She recounts an instance where a patient named Martha began to sing in response to conversation about Christmas, highlighting the potential for arts to reconnect people who have otherwise withdrawn into silence. The article defines 'the arts' broadly as any medium that can be used for creative self-expression and discusses the various types of dementia, noting their prevalence among older adults and those with conditions like multiple sclerosis or Down's syndrome.

Basting emphasizes that the arts offer a non-verbal means of expression, enabling people with dementia to communicate and connect with others even when rational language and memory have diminished. She explores two models of arts programs for individuals with dementia: medical (facilitated by certified therapists) and social (implemented by anyone with an interest in creative expression). Both approaches aim to enhance quality of life through skill-building, community engagement, and the production of meaningful art.

The author provides examples of effective arts programs like 'Memories in the Making' and 'TimeSlips,' illustrating how these initiatives can be tailored to individual interests and passions. She concludes by advocating for a combined approach that leverages both medical and social models to provide comprehensive support and enrichment for individuals with dementia.
