## Graphic Medicine

The chapter on 'Graphic Medicine' explores the integration of comics into healthcare contexts and education. It begins by tracing the origin of the term 'graphic medicine,' coined in 2008 by Ian Williams, who observed that graphic narratives could play a significant role in health discourse after reading Brian Fies's 'Mom’s Cancer.' The text elaborates on how comics can be particularly effective when there is a high density of information to convey, it is highly important, and the reader is under stress. It highlights the unique ability of comics to engage readers efficiently and appeals visually, making them an alternative yet effective learning tool in health education. Additionally, the chapter discusses the historical use of underground comics addressing stigmatized issues in healthcare and their potential for social and political activism within this field. The authors also emphasize the importance of community-building through graphic medicine both online and at academic conferences. The practical applications include using graphic narratives in professional training to enhance skills like empathy and communication, as well as engaging patients via patient education materials and support groups. This approach has been shown to improve understanding, ease transitions into clinical settings, and increase engagement among trainees.
