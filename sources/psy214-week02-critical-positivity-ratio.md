## Critical Positivity Ratio

The critical positivity ratio, also known as the 'Losada ratio' or 'Losada line,' is a concept in positive psychology that posited an exact ratio of positive to negative emotions distinguishing 'flourishing' from 'languishing' individuals. Barbara Fredrickson and Marcial Losada introduced this idea in their 2005 paper, which proposed that a positivity-to-negativity ratio of approximately 3:1 defined the threshold between these states. The concept gained significant attention among both academics and the public despite criticisms regarding its empirical foundation and mathematical validity. Critics like Nick Brown, Alan Sokal, and Harris Friedman pointed out severe flaws in Fredrickson and Losada's model, including issues with the application of differential equations and unsupported assumptions about emotional dynamics. These critiques led to a retraction by American Psychologist for the mathematical aspects of the original paper. The case highlights the importance of rigorous scientific scrutiny and has influenced discussions around the reliability and validity of research in positive psychology.
