## HEALTH HUMANITIES AND THE CREATIVE DISCIPLINES

Victoria Tischler's chapter explores the relationship between creative practices and healthcare within the context of health humanities. It discusses how creatives, such as visual artists and musicians, engage with healthcare training and delivery but face challenges due to the dominance of biomedicine in Western healthcare systems. The author highlights that creativity is often instrumentalized, viewed as a therapeutic intervention rather than an intrinsic human activity contributing to self-actualization. Tischler argues for the broader societal benefits of arts activities beyond individual well-being, such as fostering civic pride and social cohesion.

The chapter also delves into historical instances where creative development has been supported by clinical or care contexts, illustrating how artists with mental health conditions have benefitted from artistic expression within healthcare settings. Examples include W.A.F. Browne's collaboration with William Bartholomew at Crichton Royal Institution and Mary Barnes’ therapeutic community experience under R.D. Laing’s guidance. These collaborations not only provided creative opportunities but also contributed to the recovery of patients through meaningful engagement in art.

Overall, Tischler emphasizes the need for health humanities to play a more prominent role in training and practice for creatives, advocating for co-creative practices that recognize the value of arts beyond mere interventions.
