## Consonance and Pitch

This paper challenges the prevailing theories of consonance and dissonance in music, particularly those based on frequency relationships between harmonics proposed by Helmholtz and Stumpf. The authors present experimental evidence that dissonance is more closely related to pitch perception errors than previously thought. Through a series of experiments involving chord familiarity and recognition, they propose a new theory where long-term memory templates for familiar chords assist in the accurate perception of pitches, leading to reduced dissonance when these templates are activated successfully. When such mechanisms fail, dissonance arises due to cognitive incongruence between perceived pitch estimates and actual waveform periods. This study highlights the role of cognition and familiarity in auditory processing and has implications for understanding music perception, learning processes, and sensory perception more broadly.
