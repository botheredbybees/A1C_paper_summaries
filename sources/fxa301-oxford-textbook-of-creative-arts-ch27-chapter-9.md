## Psychophysiological links between cultural activities and public health

This chapter explores the physiological mechanisms that could mediate the health effects of cultural activities. Neurobiological research indicates that childhood cultural experiences can influence emotional development alongside cognitive skills. The text discusses how emotionally charged stimuli are transmitted through two brain routes: a faster, lower route and a slower, higher route, impacting the amygdala's role in stress reactions. It also mentions 'emotional competence,' where rational understanding and emotional impulses interact differently within various brain regions crucial for health. Additionally, it highlights early life training in emotional skills as essential for later social and emotional competencies, citing studies on foetal auditory learning and children’s responses to cultural activities. The chapter concludes that music and other forms of art can improve emotional engagement and social competence.
