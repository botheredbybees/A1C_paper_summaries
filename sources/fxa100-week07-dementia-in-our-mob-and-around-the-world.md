## Dementia in Our Mob and around the World

This document discusses dementia among Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander populations, highlighting both its prevalence and unique characteristics. It outlines various types of dementia, including Alzheimer's disease, vascular dementia, and other less common forms such as Lewy body dementia and alcohol-related dementia. The text emphasizes that while genetic factors can play a role in the development of certain types of dementia, late-onset Alzheimer’s disease is more commonly associated with older age.

The document highlights significant disparities in dementia prevalence among Indigenous populations compared to non-Indigenous communities. Studies from Australia reveal higher rates of dementia among Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people, particularly those residing in remote areas. For instance, the Kimberley region study found a rate nearly five times higher than that of the general Australian population (12.4% vs 2.4%). Moreover, research indicates that Indigenous males may be more affected by dementia compared to females within these populations.

The document also explores cultural and social factors influencing perceptions and management of dementia among Indigenous communities. Misconceptions about Alzheimer's disease are prevalent, particularly among younger people who have little knowledge or awareness about the condition. Additionally, traditional healers and spiritual approaches often play a role in care practices for those affected by dementia within these communities.

Comparative studies across different Indigenous populations worldwide indicate variable rates of dementia prevalence. For example, while some First Nations groups in Canada exhibit increased rates of younger onset dementia compared to non-Indigenous populations, lower rates have been observed among certain isolated Amazonian tribes and Native American communities. This variability underscores the need for culturally sensitive research methodologies and care practices that address diverse community contexts.
