## Cognitive training and cognitive rehabilitation for mild to moderate Alzheimer's disease and vascular dementia

This systematic review by Bahar-Fuchs, Clare, and Woods (2013) evaluates the effectiveness of cognitive training and rehabilitation for individuals with mild to moderate Alzheimer's disease and vascular dementia. The authors conducted a comprehensive analysis of existing studies to determine if such interventions could improve cognitive functions, mood, and daily living activities in these populations.

The review encompasses various outcomes including changes in global cognition, orientation, memory, executive function, fluency, attention, working memory, participant mood, capacity for activities of daily living (ADL), and carer-reported burden. The studies were analyzed to assess short-term impacts immediately post-intervention as well as medium-term effects up to 12 months after the intervention.

Key findings indicate that cognitive training can lead to significant improvements in certain cognitive domains, particularly memory and executive function, though results vary across different measures. Additionally, there is evidence suggesting that such interventions may enhance participants' mood and reduce carer-reported burdens, although more research is needed to confirm these effects consistently.

This review underscores the potential benefits of tailored cognitive training programs for individuals suffering from mild to moderate Alzheimer's disease or vascular dementia, emphasizing the importance of continued research to refine these practices. Such findings are pertinent to creative arts and health disciplines as they highlight the significance of personalized therapeutic approaches in enhancing quality of life for dementia patients.
