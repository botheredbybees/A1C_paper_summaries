## Know Thyself and Become What You Are: A Eudaimonic Approach to Psychological Well-Being

This article revisits Aristotle’s concept of eudaimonia, or the highest good achievable by human action, and challenges prevailing conceptions of subjective well-being that focus primarily on feelings of happiness, contentment, and life satisfaction. The authors argue that these traditional measures fail to capture the essence of eudaimonia as a pursuit of excellence based on one’s unique potential for personal growth and fulfillment. By drawing on Aristotle’s Nichomachean Ethics, existential philosophy, utilitarianism, and humanistic psychology, they construct a multidimensional model of psychological well-being (PWB) that emphasizes autonomy, environmental mastery, purpose in life, self-acceptance, positive relations with others, and personal growth. The article also discusses empirical evidence supporting the factorial validity of this PWB model, highlighting how socioeconomic contexts influence opportunities for eudaimonic well-being, and suggesting potential health benefits associated with a fulfilling and meaningful life.
