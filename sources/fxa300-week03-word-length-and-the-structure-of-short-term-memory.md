## Word length and the structure of short-term memory

The study explores the hypothesis that immediate memory span varies with word length, rather than being a constant number of chunks as suggested by Miller (1956). Experiments were conducted to test this hypothesis using different sets of words varying in syllable count and temporal duration. Results showed that memory span is inversely related to word length across various materials and conditions, contradicting the item-based hypothesis proposed by Miller. The study also investigated the influence of articulatory suppression on recall performance, finding that the effect disappears with visual presentation but remains with auditory presentation. These findings support a phonemically-based store functioning as an output buffer for speech production and supplementing working memory systems. This research is relevant to understanding the cognitive processes involved in language processing and memory retention, which can inform creative arts and health practices such as music therapy and educational interventions.
