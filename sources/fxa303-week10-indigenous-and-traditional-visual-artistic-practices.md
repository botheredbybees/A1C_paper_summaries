## Indigenous and Traditional Visual Artistic Practices: Implications for Art Therapy Clinical Practice and Research

The paper by Kaimal and Arslanbek presents a review of the therapeutic role of traditional and indigenous forms of visual artistic practice in promoting physical health and psychosocial well-being, particularly within the context of art therapy. The authors argue that artistic expression has historically evolved as an integral part of human civilizations and serves significant adaptive purposes such as evoking emotions, problem-solving, imagination, memorializing life events, and non-verbal communication. They note a lack of examination regarding how these practices can help individuals cope with mental health challenges and strengthen their sense of community.

The modern profession of art therapy has Western origins but is increasingly recognizing the value of incorporating indigenous knowledge and traditional healing practices from around the world to address global mental health needs effectively. The paper highlights the need for culturally sensitive approaches in art therapy that respect local traditions and cultural nuances while fostering a broader understanding of diverse artistic expressions.

Kaimal and Arslanbek also discuss the importance of integrating traditional wisdom into contemporary therapeutic methods, emphasizing the interconnectedness with nature and the spiritual dimensions of creative practices. They call for further research to explore how indigenous and traditional art forms can enrich clinical practice in art therapy and contribute to mental health interventions globally.
