## Why Teachers and Learners of Medicine Need Portraiture

The article by Maxwell F. Lydiatt and William M. Lydiatt examines how incorporating portraiture into medical education can enhance the teaching and learning process for both teachers and learners in health professions. Portraiture is defined as a collaborative process between subject and artist, which emphasizes careful observation, acceptance of ambiguity, and empathetic understanding—core skills also crucial in medicine. The authors argue that engaging with portraits can help trainees develop perceptual skills, navigate uncertainty, and recognize their biases when interpreting clinical information or patient interactions. This method facilitates learners' exploration of multiple perspectives, enhances self-awareness, and promotes empathy—a key component for effective healthcare delivery. Additionally, the practice of portraiture encourages clinicians to observe patients with a keen eye, akin to how surgeons scrutinize surgical sites, thereby improving decision-making under uncertainty.
