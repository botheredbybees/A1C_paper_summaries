## Anatomically distinct dopamine release during anticipation and experience of peak emotion to music

This study explores the role of dopamine in experiencing pleasure from music, a non-essential yet universally valued abstract stimulus. Using positron emission tomography (PET) and functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), researchers investigated whether intense emotional responses to pleasurable music are mediated by dopaminergic activity in specific brain regions. Participants experienced chills—a reliable physiological marker of peak emotional pleasure—as they listened to self-selected highly enjoyable musical pieces, while neutral or less engaging music served as control stimuli. The study confirmed that dopamine is released in the striatum during the experience of intense pleasure from music and identified distinct anatomical pathways for anticipation (caudate) and peak emotional response (nucleus accumbens). These findings suggest a neurochemical basis for the rewarding aspects of musical engagement, highlighting its significance across cultures and emphasizing the complex interplay between anticipation and experiencing rewards.
