## The development of metacognitive skills: evidence from observational analysis of young children’s behavior during problem-solving

This study explores the development of metacognitive skills in young children aged 5 to 7 years through an observational analysis method that captures both verbal and non-verbal behavior during problem-solving tasks. The research aims to distinguish between quantitative improvements (doing more of the same) and qualitative improvements (employing different, sophisticated skills). Findings indicate a significant increase in metacognitive skills with age, particularly in monitoring processes which reach maturity earlier than control processes. Control processes are influenced by both age and task-specific ability, while monitoring is primarily affected by general cognitive development rather than specific task proficiency. The study highlights the importance of employing observation-based methodologies to capture genuine developmental changes, addressing previous limitations due to reliance on language-heavy assessments.
