## Cognitive Reserve in Ageing and Alzheimer's Disease

Yaakov Stern's article in The Lancet Neurology discusses the concept of cognitive reserve, which explains individual differences in susceptibility to age-related brain changes or Alzheimer’s disease pathology. Cognitive reserve is divided into two types: brain reserve and cognitive reserve. Brain reserve refers to structural differences in the brain that may increase tolerance to pathology, while cognitive reserve pertains to differences in how tasks are performed that enable resilience against brain changes. Epidemiological studies suggest that higher educational and occupational attainment, as well as engaging in leisure activities, reduce the risk of developing Alzheimer’s disease by fostering greater cognitive reserve. However, once clinically diagnosed with Alzheimer's, patients with high cognitive reserve show a more rapid decline compared to those with low cognitive reserve.
