## What does autism look like in the brain?

Dr. Jeff Anderson and colleagues at the University of Utah Health investigated neural mechanisms underlying symptoms associated with autism spectrum disorder (ASD). The study, published in JAMA Network Open, utilized functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) to examine brain connectivity duration in male participants aged 19-34 years, half of whom had ASD diagnoses. Additional data from the Autism Brain Imaging Data Exchange (ABIDE) study confirmed initial findings.

The research demonstrated that individuals with autism exhibit longer-lasting neural connections compared to neurotypical controls, suggesting a difficulty in rapidly switching between mental processes. This finding may explain why people on the autism spectrum often experience distress when exposed to multiple stimuli simultaneously. The severity of social dysfunction correlated with increased synchronized activity within brain scans for those diagnosed with ASD.

The study highlights a new perspective into understanding the neurological basis of autism, offering potential avenues for developing more targeted treatment strategies and medications that could alleviate symptoms associated with the disorder.
