## Eudaimonic well-being: Early humanistic approaches

This document explores the concept of eudaimonic well-being through an examination of early humanistic psychological theories, particularly those proposed by Abraham Maslow regarding self-actualization. The text outlines Maslow’s hierarchy of needs, differentiating between deficiency (D) and being or 'growth' (B) needs, which include physiological, safety, love/belonging, esteem, self-actualization, cognitive, aesthetic, and transcendence needs from the base to the peak. A key focus is on the characteristics of self-actualized individuals, such as efficient perception, acceptance, freshness in appreciation, spontaneity, creativity, peak experiences, autonomy, detachment, resistance to enculturation, Gemeinschaftsgefuhl (a feeling of community), deep interpersonal relations, philosophical humor, problem-centering, democratic character structure, and the ability to discriminate between means and ends. The document suggests that only about 1% of the population achieves self-actualization and discusses the importance of detailed case studies for understanding this state.
