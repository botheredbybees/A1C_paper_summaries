## Ancient Texts: Exploring Medieval Remedies for Modern Pharmacology

Christina Lee's chapter in 'The Routledge Companion to Health Humanities' explores the potential of medieval medical remedies for modern pharmacology and medicine, challenging prevailing notions that pre-Enlightenment era cures were ineffective or primitive. Lee argues that medieval medicine was holistic, integrating spiritual practices like prayer and chants with biological treatments, creating a multifaceted approach to healing that considered both patient and healer in the treatment process. The chapter highlights the importance of narrative and dramatization in medieval medical practice, suggesting these elements served multiple purposes such as calming patients, teaching remedy application, and facilitating ritualistic engagement. Lee also discusses the historical context of medieval medicine within monastic institutions and questions about its efficacy and relevance to modern research, especially concerning antimicrobial resistance. The text concludes by noting recent scientific interest in traditional remedies and the formation of multidisciplinary teams to study these ancient medical texts for potential modern applications.
