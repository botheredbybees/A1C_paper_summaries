## Why Med Schools Are Requiring Students to Take Art Classes, and How It Makes Med Students Better Doctors

This article discusses the increasing trend of medical schools incorporating art classes into their curricula to enhance students' empathy, communication skills, and patient interaction. Dr. Michael Flanagan from Penn State's College of Medicine presents a seminar titled 'Impressionism and the Art of Communication,' which uses impressionist paintings by Vincent van Gogh and Claude Monet to teach medical students about mental illness and cognitive biases. The aim is to help future doctors develop better bedside manners and improve patient satisfaction through more effective communication techniques. Another professor, Dr. Paul Haidet, also integrates jazz music into his curriculum to demonstrate the importance of listening and improvisation in doctor-patient interactions. These courses are seen as a means to counteract the dehumanizing effects of technological advancements in healthcare by fostering emotional intelligence and deeper human connections. The article highlights how such initiatives can contribute positively to patient outcomes and underscores the value of interdisciplinary education for medical professionals.
