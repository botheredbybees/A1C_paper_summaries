## Motion, emotion and empathy in aesthetic experience

This paper by Freedberg and Gallese explores the role of embodied simulation in aesthetic experiences, particularly with visual arts. They argue against the primacy of cognition in responses to art and emphasize the importance of universal embodied mechanisms that activate sensations, emotions, and actions when viewing images or artworks. The authors discuss how historical and cultural contexts do not negate the significance of empathetic neural processes involved in understanding visual artworks. Through examples such as Michelangelo's Prisoners and Goya's Disasters of War, they illustrate bodily empathy in viewers' responses to art, suggesting that these physical reactions are essential for comprehending the effectiveness of both everyday images and artistic works. The study highlights the role of mirror neurons and embodied simulation in enabling empathetic understanding of others’ actions, emotions, and sensations, thereby enriching our appreciation of visual arts.
