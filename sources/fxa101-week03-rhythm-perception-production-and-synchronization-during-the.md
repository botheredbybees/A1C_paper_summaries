## Rhythm perception, production, and synchronization during the perinatal period

This review article by Provasi, Anderson, and Barbu-Roth (2014) examines the capacity for sensorimotor synchronization (SMS) in young children, infants, newborns, and fetuses. SMS refers to coordinating rhythmic movement with external rhythm, which plays a critical role in human interaction and communication. The review highlights that infants and even newborns can exhibit some level of interaction between movement and sound, particularly within their spontaneous motor tempo range. Research indicates that the interaction between auditory stimuli and motor responses is present at birth but typically operates effectively under specific conditions such as rhythms close to an individual’s natural pace. For instance, studies show young children from 18 months to four years can synchronize finger tapping with external rhythms only when these rhythms closely match their spontaneous motor tempo (around 400 ms). Older children demonstrate improved synchronization accuracy and a wider range of tempi adaptation. Social context also enhances synchronization capabilities in younger age groups. The review underscores the importance of SMS in early life for facilitating communication and interaction, suggesting potential avenues for future research on its role in developmental processes.
