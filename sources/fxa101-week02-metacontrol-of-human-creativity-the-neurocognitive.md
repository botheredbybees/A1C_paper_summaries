## Metacontrol of human creativity: The neurocognitive mechanisms of convergent and divergent thinking

This article reviews the neurocognitive mechanisms underlying divergent and convergent thinking, key components of creativity. Divergent thinking involves generating multiple solutions to a problem with flexibility and novelty, while convergent thinking focuses on finding one correct solution through persistence and analytical reasoning. The study discusses how these processes are associated with different neural networks, mood states, and neurochemical systems such as the dopamine system. Notably, divergent thinking is often linked to right frontal lobe activity and increased alpha band EEG activity, whereas convergent thinking involves left frontal regions and focused attention. Despite some overlap in neural patterns, especially under certain conditions like microdosing psilocybin, distinct mechanisms for each type of thinking are proposed. The authors suggest that creative cognition benefits from a balance between flexibility and persistence regulated by metacontrol states, which can be influenced by cortical brain activation patterns.
