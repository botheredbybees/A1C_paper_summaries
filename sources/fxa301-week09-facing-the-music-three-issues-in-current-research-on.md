## Facing the music: three issues in current research on singing and aphasia

This opinion article by Stahl and Kotz discusses three critical issues in current research on singing as a therapeutic approach for individuals with aphasia following left-hemisphere stroke. The authors first address the issue of articulatory tempo, noting that while slowing down articulation through singing can improve syllable production, it complicates clinical research due to varying speech tempos. They then explore design flaws in clinical studies investigating music-based therapy for aphasia, emphasizing the difficulty of isolating specific therapeutic elements when multiple stimuli are involved. Lastly, they highlight the role of formulaic language in successful therapies like Melodic Intonation Therapy (MIT), suggesting that common phrases engage bilateral neural networks and may be more effective than novel word sequences. The article underscores the need for careful research design to better understand the efficacy and underlying mechanisms of music-based interventions in aphasia therapy.
