## AGE AND SEX DO NOT BIAS THE USE OF ANGIOTENSIN-CONVERTING ENZYME INHIBITORS IN ACUTE MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION AND CONGESTIVE HEART FAILURE

The study investigates whether age and sex biases influence the use of angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors in patients with acute myocardial infarction (AMI) or congestive heart failure (CHF). The research was conducted at a district general hospital in London, United Kingdom. A total of 669 patients were included from April 1996 to March 1997 and followed for one year until March 1998. Patients aged over 75 years and those with AMI or CHF did not show a bias against receiving ACE inhibitors compared to younger counterparts. The study found that male and female patients were equally likely to receive these medications, regardless of their age. However, despite evidence supporting the efficacy of ACE inhibitors in reducing cardiovascular mortality for older individuals with heart conditions, only about 40% to 60% of eligible patients received this therapy. This research highlights the need for further studies to confirm whether such biases persist and suggests potential improvements in healthcare practices for treating elderly populations.
