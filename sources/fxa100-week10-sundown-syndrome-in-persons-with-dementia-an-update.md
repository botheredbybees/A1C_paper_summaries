## Sundown Syndrome in Persons with Dementia: An Update

Sundown syndrome, a common clinical phenomenon among demented individuals, involves the emergence or exacerbation of neuropsychiatric symptoms in the late afternoon and evening. The study discusses sundowning's various manifestations such as confusion, agitation, anxiety, aggression, wandering, and hallucinations. Despite its prevalence, the exact definition and etiology remain debated, with no consensus on whether it is a distinct clinical entity separate from other conditions like dementia or delirium. The review highlights that sundown syndrome may be associated with impaired circadian rhythmicity, environmental factors, and cognitive decline, potentially mediated by neurophysiological changes like suprachiasmatic nucleus degeneration and reduced melatonin production. Treatment options discussed include bright light therapy, melatonin supplements, acetylcholinesterase inhibitors, N-methyl-d-aspartate receptor antagonists, antipsychotics, and behavioral modifications aimed at alleviating symptoms and improving patient wellbeing and caregiver burden.
