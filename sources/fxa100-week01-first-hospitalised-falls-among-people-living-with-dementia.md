## First Hospitalised Falls Among People Living With Dementia: Risk Factors and Outcomes

This study, conducted by the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, investigates the first hospitalised falls among people living with dementia. It highlights that individuals with dementia have a higher risk of falling and experiencing severe outcomes compared to those without dementia. The research focuses on nearly 19,600 people who were hospitalized due to their first fall in 2019, alongside a comparison group of about 113,000 individuals who had no record of such falls.

Key findings reveal that high blood pressure and polypharmacy are prevalent risk factors for hospitalised falls among those with dementia. Additionally, balance issues and the use of nervous system drugs were significant contributors to first fall risk in community-dwelling dementia patients, while agitation and medications for depression (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) were major factors in residential aged care settings.

The study also underscores the impact of hospitalisation on individuals with dementia, noting that nearly 90% of those hospitalized due to a fall had recorded injuries. Furthermore, about 40% of community-dwelling patients entered residential aged care following their first hospitalised fall, highlighting the need for targeted interventions aimed at preventing falls and reducing injury severity.

These findings are crucial for developing tailored prevention strategies and interventions that can mitigate risks specific to dementia patients’ living environments, thus enhancing overall health outcomes in creative arts and health initiatives.
