## Dentistry and Dental Care in Antiquity: Part 2 – Egypt and the Graeco-Roman World

This study explores dental practices in ancient Egypt and the Graeco-Roman world, focusing on the evolution of dental care from the arid climate's preservation of skeletal remains to textual and archaeological evidence. In ancient Egypt, excessive tooth wear was prevalent due to dietary factors, while periodontal disease existed but caries were infrequent. Oral hygiene routines remain unclear, though natron pellets used in purification rituals may have had oral health benefits. The existence of a dedicated dental profession is debated; operative or interventive dentistry evidence is scarce, with claims often challenged by conflicting interpretations and lack of precise documentation. In the Graeco-Roman period, texts like the Hippocratic Corpus and writings of Pliny and Galen offer more detailed descriptions of surgical extractions, prosthetics, and pharmaceutical remedies, despite limited archaeological confirmation. The study highlights both advancements and limitations in ancient dental care, providing insights into cultural practices influencing modern dentistry.
