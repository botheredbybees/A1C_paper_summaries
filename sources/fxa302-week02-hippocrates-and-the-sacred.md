## Hippocrates and the Sacred

This chapter explores the relationship between Hippocrates and sacred beliefs in ancient Greek medicine. It clarifies that 'Hippocrates' can refer to both a historical figure and a collection of medical writings attributed to him. The text argues that despite criticisms against magico-religious practices, these doctors did not reject traditional religion outright but maintained a nuanced stance towards the divine. Specifically, it discusses how Hippocratic texts treat epilepsy as the ‘sacred disease’ (ἱερῆς νοῦσον), which was recognized by medical practitioners but also had religious connotations in popular usage. The chapter suggests that while there is rationalist criticism of supernatural explanations for diseases, traditional sanctuary religion and divine notions remain important within Hippocratic medicine.
