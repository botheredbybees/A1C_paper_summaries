## Musical Expectancy and Thrills

The chapter by Huron and Margulis explores the central role of expectation in music, particularly its influence on emotional responses. They argue that while other arts often evoke emotions through representation of real-world phenomena, music achieves this effect through abstract tone sequences structured over time. The authors discuss various theories of musical expectation, including those by Meyer, Narmour, Margulis, and Huron himself. Key findings include the importance of temporal aspects in emotional responses to music and the role of statistical learning in forming expectations. The chapter also delves into biological origins of prediction as a survival mechanism, highlighting how anticipatory mechanisms are crucial for rapid behavioral responses. Finally, it examines the phenomenon of frisson, or 'chills', experienced during emotionally intense moments in music. This study underscores the intricate relationship between musical structure and emotional experience, offering significant implications for both creative arts and health studies.
