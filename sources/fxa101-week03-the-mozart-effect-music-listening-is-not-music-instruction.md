## The Mozart Effect: Music Listening is Not Music Instruction

This article discusses the distinction between music listening and music instruction in relation to cognitive benefits, particularly focusing on spatial-temporal abilities. The authors clarify that the term 'Mozart effect' was originally used to describe a temporary enhancement of spatial skills after listening to Mozart's piano sonata K.448 but has been conflated with research on the effects of learning music. They argue that while brief exposure to Mozart does not significantly impact intelligence, participating in structured music education can yield long-term cognitive improvements including enhanced arithmetic and hand-eye coordination skills. The authors emphasize that such benefits from music instruction are not 'free'—they require dedicated practice and engagement.
