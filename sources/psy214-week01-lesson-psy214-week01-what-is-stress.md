## Understanding Stress: Biopsychosocial Model and Self-Care Approaches

The concept of stress in human psychology emerged in the scientific literature in the 1920s but gained common use from the 1970s. Stress is experienced universally and can manifest as mood swings, sleep disruption, fatigue, changes in appetite or activity levels, and relationship tensions. This unit introduces the biopsychosocial model used by health psychologists to understand health influences. The model considers biological, psychological, and socio-environmental factors that interact to affect overall well-being. Unlike the biomedical model, which focuses on diagnosis and treatment of diseases without addressing underlying causes, the biopsychosocial model recognizes chronic lifestyle diseases influenced by stress. Biological components include the stress response, inflammation, oxidative stress, and immune suppression. Psychological elements encompass appraisals, habitual thought patterns, and coping styles, with self-care practices aiming to build personal psychological resources. Social factors involve support networks, cultural influences, employment conditions, socioeconomic status, access to education, fresh foods, and green space.
