## Adult Neuroplasticity: More Than 40 Years of Research

This review article discusses the significant changes in our understanding of adult neuroplasticity over the past four decades, emphasizing that the mature vertebrate brain is highly adaptable and capable of structural and functional modifications throughout life. Key areas covered include neuron morphology alterations in response to stress, neuronal network adjustments including dendritic plasticity, and neurogenesis in adult brains. The authors trace historical developments since Santiago Ramón y Cajal's pioneering work, highlighting how research has moved beyond the traditional view that adult brain cells are fixed and non-renewable. Stress-induced changes such as dendritic retraction in hippocampal neurons and enhanced arborization of pyramidal neurons in the amygdala exemplify the brain’s capacity for structural remodeling. The review also addresses misconceptions regarding stress-induced neuron death, clarifying that adverse conditions like chronic stress lead to tissue alterations rather than direct cell loss. Additionally, it explores neurogenesis, challenging the longstanding belief against new neuronal generation in adult brains and emphasizing its relevance for understanding diseases such as Parkinson's and multiple sclerosis.
