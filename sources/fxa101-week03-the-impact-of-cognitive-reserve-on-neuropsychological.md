## The impact of cognitive reserve on neuropsychological measures in clinical trials

This chapter examines the concept of cognitive reserve (CR) and its implications for neuropsychological measures in clinical trials. CR refers to individual differences in brain capacity that allow some individuals to maintain higher levels of cognitive function despite neurological pathology compared to others with similar conditions. The authors discuss two main models of CR: threshold theory, which posits a critical level of brain reserve must be exceeded before functional deficits become apparent; and active model theories emphasizing the brain's ability to adapt and compensate in response to changing demands. The chapter also highlights how premorbid factors such as education and intelligence levels influence neuropsychological performance, often more so than disease state or treatment status. It underscores the importance of considering CR when interpreting clinical trial data involving cognitive assessments, particularly for high-functioning individuals where standard measures may not accurately reflect cognitive decline. Examples from cardiovascular disease, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and dementia illustrate how CR impacts neuropsychological outcomes in various health conditions.
