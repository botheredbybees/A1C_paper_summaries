## Bodily Responses to Music

Chapter 10 of the book 'Music in the Human Experience: An Introduction to Music Psychology' by Donald A. Hodges and Gary E. McPherson explores the physical and physiological responses humans experience when engaging with music. The chapter begins by asking readers to reflect on memorable musical experiences, noting common reactions such as crying, laughing, feeling goosebumps, or experiencing changes in heart rate. Researchers have long been fascinated by these bodily responses, which are categorized into two main types: physiological (internal processes like heart rate and blood pressure) and physical (external behaviors observable without instrumentation). The chapter discusses numerous studies that examine how music affects the body's internal systems, finding no consensus on whether stimulative or sedative music uniformly alters physiological states. Key variables influencing these responses include personal listening history, emotional context, listener characteristics, and testing environment conditions. Despite inconsistencies in research findings due to varied methodologies and individual differences, the chapter underscores the profound impact of music on human physiology.
