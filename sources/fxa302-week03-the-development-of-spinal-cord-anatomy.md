## The Development of Spinal Cord Anatomy

This paper traces the historical development of spinal cord anatomy from ancient Mesopotamian and Greek times through to advancements in the 18th and 19th centuries. It begins with an examination of early illustrations showing injuries that implied understanding of spinal cord function, such as The Dying Lioness depicting a lioness with spinal injury. Contributions by notable figures like Galen who dissected animals to study nerves, and later anatomists including Gerard Blasius who accurately illustrated the anatomy of many animal species, are discussed. Further progress was made during the 18th century by scientists such as Johann Jacob Huber and Felix Vicq d’Azyr, who refined understanding of spinal cord structure into specific columns and bundles. In the 19th century, studies focused on identifying different tracts in the spinal cord, with Luigi Rolando pioneering research on spinal nerves and their functions. The work culminates in detailing Sir Charles Bell's discovery that motor impulses travel through anterior roots while sensory signals use posterior roots, fundamentally advancing knowledge of neurological pathways.
