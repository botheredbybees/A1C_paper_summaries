## Neuroanatomy, Limbic System

The document provides an overview of the limbic system's structure and function within the brain, emphasizing its role in regulating emotions, memory, motivation, and social processing. The limbic system is a collection of brain structures located near the thalamus, beneath the cerebral cortex, and above the brainstem. It comprises mesencephalic, diencephalic, and telencephalic components that originate from different embryological sources and contribute to various neurological functions. Key structures include the hypothalamus, hippocampus, amygdala, cingulate gyrus, and entorhinal cortex. Each of these structures plays a specific role in memory formation, emotional responses, learning, and cognitive processes. For instance, lesions or damage to the hippocampus can result in anterograde amnesia as seen in the case study of 'H.M.', while disorders like schizophrenia are associated with asymmetry and volume loss in limbic system components. Additionally, research suggests that the limbic system's traditional model may need revision, proposing a more complex network involving distinct pathways for memory, emotion-cognition association, and autobiographical reflection.
