## Narrativity and medicine: some critical reflections

The article 'Narrativity and medicine: some critical reflections' by Rolf Ahlzén critically examines the growing interest in narrative and narrativity within medical humanities, particularly focusing on the concept of narrative medicine. Since the late 20th century, there has been significant attention given to narratives as essential for understanding personal lives, ethical values, and human suffering. This narrative turn influenced medicine through Rita Charon's narrative medicine project, which aimed at enhancing physicians' narrative competence to better address patients' subjective experiences.

However, more recent critiques have raised doubts about the foundational assumptions of this movement, questioning whether narratives truly provide a unique insight into personal perspectives or suffer from overreaching ambitions. The article explores arguments from critics like Galen Strawson and Angela Woods who challenge the extent to which narrative understanding is central to human experience.

Despite these criticisms, Ahlzén argues that narrative medicine remains a promising approach within medical humanities for emphasizing the importance of individual subjectivity in clinical practice, albeit with more modest ambitions. The article emphasizes the need for caution in applying overly broad claims about narrativity's role in medicine while recognizing its potential to enhance patient-centered care.
