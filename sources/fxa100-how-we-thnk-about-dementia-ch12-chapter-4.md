## How We Think about Dementia: Personhood, Rights, Ethics, the Arts and What They Mean for Care

The chapter explores philosophical perspectives on dementia, emphasizing theories of personhood and identity as proposed by philosophers like Merleau-Ponty, Bourdieu, Taylor, and Matthews. It discusses the concept of 'embodied selfhood' developed by Kontos, which posits that personal identity is not solely conscious but also resides in habitual bodily practices. The author argues that meaningfulness persists even in severe dementia cases, where actions are interpreted as expressions of tacit knowledge and lived experiences sedimented into habits. This leads to practical implications for caregiving, advocating for a holistic approach that respects the person's autonomy and history.
