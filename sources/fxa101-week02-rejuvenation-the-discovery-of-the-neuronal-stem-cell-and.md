## Rejuvenation: The Discovery of the Neuronal Stem Cell and Lessons for Preserving Our Brains

The chapter 'Rejuvenation: The Discovery of the Neuronal Stem Cell and Lessons for Preserving Our Brains' by Norman Doidge in 'The Brain That Changes Itself' explores the story of Dr. Stanley Karansky, a ninety-year-old who engages in various activities to keep his brain healthy and active. Despite losing his first wife to cancer and facing several health challenges including heart issues, Dr. Karansky maintains an optimistic outlook and continues learning new things, such as engaging with astronomy and rock collecting. The chapter also delves into the historical context of neuroscience research, particularly focusing on Santiago Ramón y Cajal's work which concluded that adult brains do not regenerate neurons due to their complexity. However, in 1998, Frederick 'Rusty' Gage and Peter Eriksson discovered neuronal stem cells in the human hippocampus, challenging this long-held belief and opening up new avenues for understanding brain plasticity and regeneration. This discovery has profound implications for the creative arts and health by highlighting how continued learning and engagement with novel activities can rejuvenate the brain and preserve cognitive function.
