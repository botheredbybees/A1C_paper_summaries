## Synergistic Effects of Age on Patterns of White and Gray Matter Volume across Childhood and Adolescence

This study explores the development of white and gray matter in the human brain from late childhood through adolescence, focusing on how these structures change in volume over time. The research demonstrates that while gray matter typically decreases (thins) during this period, white matter increases in volume, a pattern thought to be driven by developmental processes such as myelination and synaptic pruning. The study identifies coordinated clusters of regions within the brain where both white and gray matter show parallel age-related changes. For example, specific areas of white matter with similar fiber directions (like anterior-posterior or left-right orientations) showed consistent volumetric trends that mirrored corresponding patterns in nearby gray matter regions. This suggests a complex interplay between neural structures during development, which could be crucial for understanding typical brain maturation and the basis for neurodevelopmental disorders.
