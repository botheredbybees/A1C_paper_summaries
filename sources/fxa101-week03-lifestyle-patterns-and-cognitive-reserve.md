## Lifestyle patterns and cognitive reserve

The concept of cognitive reserve (CR) posits that innate intelligence or life experiences such as education and occupational achievements provide skills and repertoires to help individuals cope better with advancing Alzheimer's disease pathology. Epidemiological evidence indicates that engaging in intellectual, social, and physical activities slows cognitive decline in the elderly and reduces dementia risk. This theory is supported by genetic, structural, and functional imaging studies suggesting that life experiences may enhance cognitive network efficiency.

The hypothesis of CR implies individual differences in responding to AD pathology. For instance, some cognitively intact individuals have been found post-mortem to have advanced AD pathology due to larger brain volumes providing compensatory capacity. Studies show that higher educational and occupational attainment is associated with more rapid cognitive decline among AD patients but also offers a buffer against clinical expression of dementia.

Beyond traditional factors like IQ, education, and occupation, environmental influences such as lifestyle activities may provide reserve and alter the risk for cognitive decline or clinical Alzheimer's disease (AD). Research suggests that physical activity enhances neurogenesis, improves cerebral blood flow, stimulates trophic factors, and upregulates brain-derived neurotrophic factor gene expression. These findings suggest a direct physiological association between physical activity and brain health.

Cross-sectional studies generally find positive associations between engaging in stimulating activities and cognitive performance across various tasks. However, longitudinal data is crucial to determine causal directionality. Several longitudinal studies using structural equation modeling reveal that initial high intellectual functioning leads to more environmental complexity, which sustains intellectual function over time. Engaging in intellectually stimulating activities appears to buffer against cognitive decline.
