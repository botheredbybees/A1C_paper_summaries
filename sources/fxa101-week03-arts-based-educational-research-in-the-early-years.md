## Arts-based educational research in the early years

Georgina Barton's paper discusses arts-based educational research (ABER) as a method suitable for studying children in their early developmental stages. The author highlights that traditional linguistic methods of communication may not be effective with young children who are still developing language skills, whereas visual and auditory modes can serve as powerful tools for expression. Barton argues that the arts play an integral role in child development and should therefore be incorporated into educational research involving young participants.

The paper reviews several projects using ABER to study early childhood education, noting how these methods give children a voice and allow them to express complex ideas through art forms such as drawing or singing. This approach contrasts with previous practices that often viewed children merely as subjects requiring adult validation in research contexts.

Barton elaborates on the benefits of arts-based learning in early years, including physical development, social interaction skills, cognitive growth, expressive capabilities, creativity and problem-solving abilities. She also discusses specific examples where ABER was applied successfully to capture meaningful insights from young learners. Overall, the paper advocates for integrating arts-based research methods into studies involving children due to their capacity to enhance understanding of early childhood education dynamics.
