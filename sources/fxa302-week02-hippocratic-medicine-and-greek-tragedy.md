## HIPPOCRATIC MEDICINE AND GREEK TRAGEDY

The chapter 'HIPPOCRATIC MEDICINE AND GREEK TRAGEDY' by Jacques Jouanna explores the intersection between Greek medical thought and tragic drama during a period known as the Golden Age of both medicine and tragedy. The author emphasizes that Hippocratic medicine, represented primarily through the works attributed to Hippocrates (460-370 BC), marked a significant shift towards rational explanations for disease. In contrast, ancient Greek tragedies often portrayed disease in more mythic or narrative terms.

Jouanna highlights the concept of 'loimos' (pestilence or plague) as it appears in both medical texts and tragedy. He notes that while Hippocratic medicine offers detailed observations on epidemic diseases affecting populations, tragic playwrights such as Sophocles depict plagues as catastrophic events striking entire communities. Jouanna contrasts these approaches by examining the symptoms, causes, and treatments described for pestilence in 'Oedipus Rex', showing how the dramatic narrative serves a different purpose from medical documentation.

The chapter underscores the relevance of this study to creative arts and health by illustrating how both medicine and tragedy reflect societal perceptions of disease and suffering. It highlights the interconnectedness between the rational analysis of illness and its representation in art, providing insights into the cultural context of ancient Greek society.
