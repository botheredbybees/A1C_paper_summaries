## Rethinking musicality in dementia as embodied and relational

This article challenges the biomedical and pharmaceutical approaches to dementia care that often prioritize pharmacological interventions over non-pharmacological methods such as music therapy. The authors argue for a relational citizenship model, emphasizing the importance of embodiment and interdependence in understanding and supporting musicality among individuals with dementia. They assert that current understandings of musical perception are overly focused on cognitive processes and neglect the bodily experience and expression inherent in musical engagement. Through case studies from ethnographic research and elder-clowning initiatives, the authors illustrate how music can foster meaningful communication and self-expression rather than merely serving as a tool for behavioral modification or cognitive enhancement. The paper highlights the ethical imperative to support musicality comprehensively through institutional policies and practices, acknowledging its role in enhancing quality of life and social engagement for people with dementia.
