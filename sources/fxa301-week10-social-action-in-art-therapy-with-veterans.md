## Social Action in Art Therapy with Veterans

Casey Lee Davenport's master's thesis explores the application of social action and justice principles within art therapy for veteran populations. The document provides an overview of benefits associated with art therapy, theoretical assumptions underpinning its effectiveness, and a literature review focusing on specific issues faced by veterans such as PTSD, traumatic brain injury, sexual trauma, substance use, dishonorable discharge, and homelessness. Davenport discusses the transition back to civilian life after military service and how it impacts identity formation for these individuals. The thesis also examines therapeutic interventions using art therapy in healing from trauma and outlines ethical considerations within this context.

A key project described is 'I Am Not Invisible,' an initiative aimed at empowering veterans through photography and public exhibition. This project addresses the social isolation many veterans experience post-military service by creating a platform for self-expression and community engagement. Davenport's work highlights the importance of cultural sensitivity when working with diverse veteran communities, including considerations around gender and sexual orientation.

The thesis concludes by discussing broader implications for mental health practice and suggests future research directions based on initial findings from 'I Am Not Invisible.' It underscores the potential for art therapy to serve as a powerful tool in promoting social justice and community action among military veterans.
