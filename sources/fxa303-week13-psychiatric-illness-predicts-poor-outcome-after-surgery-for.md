## Psychiatric illness predicts poor outcome after surgery for hip fracture: a prospective cohort study

This study investigates the prevalence and impact of psychiatric illness on outcomes after hip fracture surgery in elderly patients. The research, conducted in two Leeds hospitals, recruited 731 participants who underwent surgery for hip fractures. Within five days post-surgery, participants were assessed using standardized diagnostic tools to detect various psychiatric conditions including dementia, delirium, depression, and alcohol misuse.

The findings reveal that psychiatric illness is highly prevalent among patients with hip fractures: 55% of participants exhibited cognitive impairment (40% had dementia, and 15% suffered from delirium), while 13% were diagnosed with depressive disorders. The study indicates a significant correlation between these mental health conditions and prolonged hospital stays as well as increased mortality rates over six months post-surgery.

Patients diagnosed with dementia or delirium faced notably higher risks of death within the six-month follow-up period compared to those without psychiatric illnesses, although depression did not correlate with an elevated risk of mortality. These results underscore the need for targeted interventions aimed at addressing psychiatric conditions in elderly hip fracture patients, highlighting the importance of these factors in determining overall health outcomes and quality of life.
