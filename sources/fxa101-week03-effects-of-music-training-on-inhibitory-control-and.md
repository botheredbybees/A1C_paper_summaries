## Effects of Music Training on Inhibitory Control and Associated Neural Networks in School-Aged Children: A Longitudinal Study

This study examines the impact of music training on inhibitory control and associated neural networks in school-aged children from underserved communities over a period of five years. The research compares three groups: children involved in music, those engaged in sports, and those with no systematic after-school programs. Inhibitory control was measured using delayed gratification, flanker, and Color-Word Stroop tasks, both inside and outside an MRI scanner. After 3 years of training, children participating in music demonstrated a preference for larger, delayed rewards over immediate smaller ones compared to the control group. Significant improvements were observed in the music group’s accuracy on the flanker task after 3 and 4 years of training but not in the sports or control groups. MRI analyses revealed that children with music training exhibited greater bilateral activation in certain brain regions during the Color-Word Stroop task, suggesting enhanced neural development related to inhibitory control. The findings indicate that structured extracurricular programs, particularly those involving music, can accelerate cognitive development and improve executive function skills in childhood.
