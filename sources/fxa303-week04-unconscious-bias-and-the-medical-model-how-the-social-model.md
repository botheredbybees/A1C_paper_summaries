## Unconscious Bias and the Medical Model: How the Social Model May Hold the Key to Transformative Thinking About Disability Discrimination

Stephen Bunbury's article explores the impact of unconscious bias and the medical model on disability discrimination, arguing for a shift towards the social model to foster transformative thinking and reduce societal discrimination against disabled individuals. The medical model views disability as an individual problem necessitating medical intervention and often leads to societal exclusion through stigma and negative stereotypes. In contrast, the social model emphasizes that societal structures and attitudes create barriers to participation, rather than the impairment itself being a cause of inequality.

Bunbury discusses how legislation such as the Equality Act 2010 in the UK and similar laws globally tend to focus on individual deficits underpinned by medical definitions. This perspective can inadvertently reinforce negative perceptions and segregation instead of promoting inclusivity and equality. He advocates for legislative frameworks to adopt a social model approach that acknowledges societal barriers as the primary impediment to disability rights and participation.

The article highlights that while reasonable adjustments are mandated in anti-discrimination laws, their effectiveness is limited if rooted in medical notions of disability. Bunbury suggests that integrating critical theories can broaden understanding and enhance strategies for addressing social exclusion faced by disabled people. He concludes that embracing the social model could lead to more effective legal approaches to combatting disability discrimination, ultimately aiming to transform societal attitudes towards inclusion.
