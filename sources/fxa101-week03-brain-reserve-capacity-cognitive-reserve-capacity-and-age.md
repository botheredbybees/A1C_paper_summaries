## Brain Reserve Capacity, Cognitive Reserve Capacity, and Age-Based Functional Plasticity After Congenital and Acquired Brain Injury in Children

This chapter explores the concepts of brain reserve capacity (BRC) and cognitive reserve capacity (CRC) in the context of childhood brain injury, emphasizing their role in functional plasticity and recovery outcomes. The authors define BRC as a hypothetical construct representing the passive capacity of the brain to withstand damage without significant functional impairment, which can be influenced by factors such as head circumference or educational level. CRC, on the other hand, refers to an individual's ability to optimize performance through efficient use of neural networks and cognitive strategies in response to increased demand. The chapter also discusses how these capacities interact with age-related variables and lesion characteristics to influence recovery outcomes post-brain injury. This work is particularly relevant for understanding resilience and adaptability in children facing neurological challenges, offering insights into how brain health can be preserved or enhanced through environmental enrichment and educational interventions.
