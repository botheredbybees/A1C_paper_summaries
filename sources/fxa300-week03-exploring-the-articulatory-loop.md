## Exploring the Articulatory Loop

This chapter by Baddeley, Lewis, and Vallar explores the role of articulatory suppression in immediate memory for auditorily presented items. The authors conduct five experiments to test whether phonological similarity effects and word length effects are influenced by articulatory processes when subjects engage in continuous speech production (articulatory suppression). They find that while articulatory suppression abolishes the word length effect if applied during both presentation and recall, it does not eliminate the phonological similarity effect. The findings suggest a complex model involving an articulatory loop with an additional phonological store responsible for the similarity effects and an articulatory rehearsal process causing the word length effect. These results have implications for understanding working memory in the context of speech and auditory processing, which is relevant to creative arts and health research focusing on cognitive functions involved in musical performance and therapy.
