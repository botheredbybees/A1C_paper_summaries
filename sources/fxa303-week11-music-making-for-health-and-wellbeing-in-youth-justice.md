## Music-making for health and wellbeing in youth justice settings

This study explores the impact of participatory music programs on young people in youth justice settings, focusing on how such interventions can foster social reintegration and support mental well-being while providing life skills and emotional resilience. The research was conducted from 2010 to 2013 with 118 participants aged 13–21 years across eight settings in England and Wales. It employs a mixed-methods approach to understand the experiences of young people engaging with music programs, particularly considering the concept of 'musical affordances' (DeNora, 2000). The study highlights that positive outcomes from music-making activities are mediated by contextual factors such as institutional rules, social relations, and issues of power and identity. It also addresses the challenges of navigating societal perceptions about certain musical genres and their potential influence on young people's behavior and identity formation. The findings underscore the importance of understanding the situated nature of these programs to enhance their effectiveness in addressing health and social needs among youth offenders.
