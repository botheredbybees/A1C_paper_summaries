## That's the Way to Do It! A History of Punch & Judy

The document 'That's the Way to Do It! A History of Punch & Judy' provides an extensive overview of the evolution and cultural significance of the traditional British puppet show featuring Mr. Punch and his wife, Judy. The history traces back to the mid-17th century when a version of Punch was first recorded by Samuel Pepys in England, performed by Italian puppeteer Pietro Gimonde, also known as Signor Bologna, who used marionettes rather than glove puppets. Over time, Punch's character and performances underwent significant transformations, becoming more integral to British culture. By the 18th century, Punch had become a celebrity figure in Britain, participating in various puppet shows and even satirical plays where he often battled with the Devil, symbolizing his subversive nature. In the 19th century, Punch transitioned from marionette stage performances to portable booths on city streets and country lanes, accompanied by his wife Judy (formerly Joan) and a dog named Toby. This period also saw the development of more elaborate costumes for Punch, moving from plain white Italian attire to the traditional red and yellow outfit symbolizing an English jester. The document highlights the continuous adaptation and cultural relevance of Punch & Judy shows in Britain, showcasing their integration into popular entertainment and social commentary over three centuries.
