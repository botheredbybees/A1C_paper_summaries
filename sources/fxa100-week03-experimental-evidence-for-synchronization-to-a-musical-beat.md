## Experimental Evidence for Synchronization to a Musical Beat in a Nonhuman Animal

This study presents experimental evidence for synchronization to a musical beat in a sulphur-crested cockatoo named Snowball, challenging the notion that rhythmic movement in response to music is uniquely human. The research involved manipulating the tempo of a song known to Snowball across various speeds and observing the bird's ability to adjust its head bobs and foot movements accordingly. Findings indicate spontaneous synchronization with musical beats over a broad range of tempi, suggesting this capability may not be exclusive to humans but could also be found in species capable of complex vocal learning, such as parrots. This discovery opens avenues for further investigation into the neurobiological underpinnings and evolutionary aspects of rhythmic movement in nonhuman animals, offering potential insights relevant to creative arts and health disciplines.
