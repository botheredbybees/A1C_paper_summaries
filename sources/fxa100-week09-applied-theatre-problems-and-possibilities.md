## Applied Theatre: Problems and Possibilities

In her article 'Applied Theatre: Problems and Possibilities,' Judith Ackroyd explores the challenges and implications of defining and categorizing applied theatre practices. She begins by discussing the difficulty in delineating the boundaries of applied theatre due to its diverse applications, ranging from community development to corporate training. Ackroyd argues that despite these differences, a common thread among practitioners is their belief in the transformative power of theatre beyond mere entertainment.

She proposes a continuum where one end represents purely functional application of theatre for specific ends and the other represents theatrical performances focused on form and artistry. The author emphasizes that understanding theatre form is essential to both ends of this spectrum, even when applied theatre aims at social or therapeutic change. This perspective contrasts with a binary distinction between traditional 'theatre' and 'applied theatre.'

Ackroyd highlights the historical use of drama for societal purposes, such as moral instruction in ancient Greek plays and modern role-play training in corporate settings. She advocates for critical evaluation of applied theatre to ensure its efficacy and ethical use. The article also touches on the need for research to understand how theatre can effectively contribute to social change while maintaining artistic integrity.

The relevance to creative arts and health is evident, as Ackroyd's work underscores the potential of theatre to address societal issues, including those related to mental and physical well-being through therapeutic applications.
