## The Arts and Dementia Care

This lesson explores the intersection of visual arts and dementia care. It examines how artists with dementia perceive the world through their artwork, noting changes in color perception and spatial errors. The work of William Utermohlen and Anne Adams is highlighted for illustrating cognitive decline. Research indicates that Alzheimer's patients may use more reds and yellows, while Lewy body dementia often involves hallucinations depicted visually. Frontotemporal dementia can impact the ability to draw faces but enhances detailed realism in other artwork. Art therapy is described as a form of psychotherapy that uses art-making for emotional and psychological well-being. Its origins date back to 1942 with Adrian Hill's tuberculosis recovery experience, evolving into recognized professional practice by the 1960s. The lesson also discusses participatory arts programs versus traditional art therapy, emphasizing social interaction over individual therapeutic goals. Studies demonstrate the positive impacts of arts programs on dementia patients' quality of life and resilience, though specific optimal practices remain to be determined.
