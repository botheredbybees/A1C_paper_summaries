## Humor and Resiliency: Towards a Process Model of Coping and Growth

Nicholas A. Kuiper's article explores the role of humor within a resilience framework, emphasizing its potential to enhance coping mechanisms and personal growth in the face of adversity or trauma. The paper begins by defining resiliency as the capacity to adapt effectively during stressful times, highlighting how positive attributes like humor can contribute to psychological well-being. It reviews both anecdotal evidence from emergency service workers who use humor to cope with high-stress situations and empirical research showing that humor is one of several character strengths (alongside kindness, leadership, love, and social intelligence) that significantly increase after major traumatic events. This contributes to overall life satisfaction and resilience.

The article contrasts traditional psychological approaches focusing on negative factors like adverse stressors or dysfunctional traits with the positive psychology perspective emphasizing personal assets for growth under adversity. It also discusses contemporary research into humor as a coping mechanism, underscoring its role in emotion regulation by distancing oneself from stressful events through cognitive reappraisals. Overall, Kuiper proposes integrating humor within resiliency theory to understand how it aids individuals in managing and growing despite traumatic experiences.
