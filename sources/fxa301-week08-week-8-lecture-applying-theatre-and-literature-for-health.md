## Week 8 Lecture – Applying Theatre and Literature for Health and Wellbeing

Dr Brendan Lamb’s Week 8 lecture on applying theatre and literature for health and wellbeing emphasizes the therapeutic benefits of these arts. The lecture begins with an overview of how engaging with theatre, particularly through laughter, has well-documented physiological and psychological health benefits such as stress reduction, improved circulation, lowered blood pressure, pain relief, and protection against depression (Louie et al., 2016). Case studies like Sharing Stories in Perth highlight the use of drama workshops to educate CaLD youth on sexual health by breaking down social barriers and fostering peer education networks.

The lecture also delves into literature and creative writing, noting that shared reading groups have gained traction for their potential to enhance well-being. Research shows that reading aloud can evoke physical responses and provide a healing power through emotional engagement (Roemhild & James, 2024; Wassiliwizky et al., 2017). A case study from the Royal Liverpool Hospital demonstrates how shared reading can relieve symptoms of chronic pain by improving psychological health and daily functional capacity. Another example is Janet Lees’ Creative Writing for Wellbeing sessions that promote self-expression, community building, and accessibility to all participants.

The lecture concludes with a brainstorming session for devising an eight-week community arts project aimed at promoting better health and wellbeing. Students are guided through planning questions that consider the target cohort, problem addressed, use of arts, activities or outcomes focus, consultation needs, venue choices, measurable outcomes, evidence-based claims, risk management, and evaluation strategies. The lecture underscores the importance of ethical and inclusive practices in delivering such projects.
