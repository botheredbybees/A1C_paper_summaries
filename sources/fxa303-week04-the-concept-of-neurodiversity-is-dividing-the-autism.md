## The Concept of Neurodiversity Is Dividing the Autism Community

Simon Baron-Cohen's article explores the debate within the autism community regarding the concept of neurodiversity. The term 'neurodiversity' was coined by Judy Singer and has gained traction as a respectful way to view neurological differences, akin to biodiversity. Proponents argue that autism should not be pathologized but rather seen as part of natural human variation. They advocate for an inclusive environment where autistic individuals can thrive.

Critics, often parents or severely affected autistic individuals, emphasize the challenges associated with autism and call for interventions aimed at preventing or curing serious impairments. This perspective is perceived by some in the neurodiversity movement as threatening to their existence, akin to eugenics.

Baron-Cohen suggests that these viewpoints are not necessarily mutually exclusive and can be reconciled by recognizing the heterogeneity within autism. He outlines various aspects of autism such as language skills, cognitive abilities, and co-occurring conditions (like epilepsy or gastrointestinal issues) which may fall under different categories like 'disorder,' 'disability,' or 'difference.' The article concludes that understanding this diversity can help in integrating both perspectives and providing appropriate support to individuals across the spectrum.
