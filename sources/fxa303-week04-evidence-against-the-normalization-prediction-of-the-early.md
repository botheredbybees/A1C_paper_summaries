## Evidence against the “normalization” prediction of the early brain overgrowth hypothesis of autism

The study challenges the 'normalization' prediction of the Early Brain Overgrowth Hypothesis in autism spectrum disorder (ASD), which posits that brain overgrowth seen in early childhood stabilizes to normal size by middle childhood and beyond. The research, conducted with large samples across different ages and genders, revealed consistent evidence of enlarged total brain volume throughout young adulthood in individuals with ASD compared to typically developing controls (TDC). Key findings include larger cerebellar, gray matter, white matter, and lateral ventricular volumes in the ASD group. There was no interaction between age or sex on these measures but a significant IQ-by-diagnosis interaction where higher IQ correlated with increased brain volume in TDCs but not in individuals with ASD. The study concludes that brain enlargement persists across development without cognitive benefits or symptom severity prediction, questioning clinical implications.
