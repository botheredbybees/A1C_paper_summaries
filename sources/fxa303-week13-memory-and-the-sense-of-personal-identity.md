## Memory and the Sense of Personal Identity

The article 'Memory and the Sense of Personal Identity' by Stanley B. Klein and Shaun Nichols discusses the relationship between memory and personal identity, focusing on how episodic memories contribute to one's sense of self-continuity over time. The authors present a case study of a neurological patient who can recall past events accurately but lacks a subjective feeling that these memories belong to him personally. This leads them to propose a two-component model for the sense of personal identity: one component provides the content of memory, and another generates the sense of ownership or 'mineness.' The paper argues that this new understanding has implications for debates about quasi-memory and supports further exploration into how memory informs philosophical theories of personal identity. It is relevant to creative arts and health fields by highlighting the psychological underpinnings of self-referential memories and their impact on mental well-being.
