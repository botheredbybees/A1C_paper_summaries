## Humour interventions for patients in palliative care—a randomized controlled trial

This randomized controlled trial evaluates the effects of a humour intervention in palliative care settings. The study, based on McGhee's humour habits programme, aimed to improve life satisfaction and mood among end-of-life patients through humor. Participants were divided into an intervention group that engaged in recalling humorous past events and producing humor, and a control group. Data collection included questionnaires assessing cheerfulness, seriousness, symptom burden, perceived stress, and saliva samples for oxytocin levels. While the intervention showed positive effects on cheerfulness and reduced seriousness and stress in the intervention group, it was too demanding for many patients due to its complexity. The study concludes that simple interventions are needed for patients with cognitive or physical limitations at end of life.
