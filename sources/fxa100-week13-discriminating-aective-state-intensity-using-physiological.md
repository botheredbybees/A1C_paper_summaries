## Discriminating aﬀective state intensity using physiological responses

The paper explores the use of physiological signals, obtained through wearable sensors, to interpret human affective states such as relaxation and stress. The authors designed a highly controlled experimental protocol using audio tracks and math calculations to induce these states in participants. They found that Galvanic Skin Response (GSR) and Photoplethysmography (PPG) could effectively distinguish between relaxation and stressful states, with varying intensities being detectable based on the type of audio player used (traditional headphones vs. immersive Sound6D pillow). The study highlights the potential for physiological data to provide a reliable measure of emotional states, contributing to the development of systems that incorporate user emotion recognition in human-machine interaction contexts. This has significant implications for creative arts and health applications, particularly in music therapy for elderly care and neurodegenerative diseases treatment.
