## Relating Physical Activity to Cognitive Function, Brain Morphology, and AD-related Blood Biomarkers: Preliminary Findings from the IGNITE study

The study by Lauren Oberlin and colleagues explores the relationship between physical activity, cognitive function, brain morphology, and Alzheimer's disease (AD) blood biomarkers in older adults. The research is part of the IGNITE study, a phase 3 clinical trial that assesses the impact of aerobic exercise on cognition and brain health over a 12-month period in 648 cognitively unimpaired individuals aged 65 to 80 years. Participants were randomized into three conditions: moderate-intensity aerobic exercise for either 150 minutes or 225 minutes per week, and light-intensity stretching/toning as a control condition.

The preliminary findings indicate that higher baseline cardiorespiratory fitness (CRF) is associated with greater hippocampal subfield volumes, preserved white matter microstructural integrity, and better cognitive performance across multiple domains. Additionally, increased moderate-to-vigorous physical activity (MVPA) was linked to better multi-domain cognitive function and decelerated brain aging. Notably, higher CRF and MVPA levels were also associated with lower neurofilament light chain (NfL) levels, particularly among individuals positive for amyloid-beta (Aβ), suggesting that maintaining high fitness may mitigate neurodegenerative changes in early-stage AD.

The study identifies several moderators of these relationships, including demographic factors, health status, and genetic markers. These findings have significant implications for developing personalized exercise prescriptions to optimize cognitive and brain health in older adults.
