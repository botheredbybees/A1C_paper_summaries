## San trance dance: embodied experience and neurological mechanisms

This article explores the San trance dance, a ritual among the Ju/'Hoansi people in the Kalahari, focusing on its neurological mechanisms and implications for shamanic rituals. The author integrates ethnographic research with findings from neuroscience to propose a model explaining the physiological and psychological changes experienced during the trance state. Physical exertion triggers sympathetic nervous system activation leading to noradrenaline release and hyperventilation, which in turn activates the amygdala, producing fear akin to being underwater. Further neurological shifts involve disruption of self/other distinctions and altered perception of faces and bodies, resulting in visionary imagery typical of San rock art. The study highlights how sustained focus from persistent hunting contributes to the trance capability and emphasizes the role of the Eland in trance-related visual representations.
