## Creativity for Health and Wellbeing in the Education System

The document discusses the critical role of creativity in promoting health and wellbeing within educational systems, emphasizing its importance amid increasing mental health challenges among children and young people. It highlights that up to one in six children aged 5-16 have diagnosable mental health disorders, with many unable to access support through NHS services. The pandemic further exacerbated these issues by causing an increase in worry, low mood, grief, and feelings of hopelessness among children, alongside rising rates of PTSD, depression, anxiety, and self-harming behaviors.

The text underscores the necessity of early intervention and prevention strategies, advocating for universal access to creative activities within schools as a means to mitigate mental health crises. Schools play a pivotal role in providing equal opportunities for arts engagement regardless of socioeconomic background, thereby reducing health inequalities. However, there has been a decline in arts education provision due to reduced funding and prioritization of core subjects linked to performance measures.

The document also emphasizes the benefits of creative activities such as music, drawing, painting, making, and reading, which have positive impacts on behavior, self-confidence, emotional regulation, relationship building, and resilience. Engaging with these activities helps young people cope with their feelings and distractions from negative thoughts, offering safe spaces for expression and facilitating friendships. Moreover, the arts contribute significantly to social skills development, reducing bullying, enhancing engagement with learning, and improving emotional competence among children facing various difficulties.
