## Making Metaphors from Photos

Nathan Lyons, a renowned photographer and educator, discusses his approach to creating diptychs and metaphorical images in this piece by Rena Silverman. Lyons explains that he begins photographing based on spontaneous responses to intriguing elements in his environment rather than following a structured plan. By pairing two seemingly unrelated photographs, Lyons creates a third meaning that resonates with viewers through the interplay of visual and contextual symbolism. For example, placing an image of a confined rocking chair next to graffiti messages about ADHD and reclaiming life transforms these images into a powerful metaphor for societal constraints on personal freedom.

Lyons' interest in metaphors was sparked during his early studies when he deviated from conventional academic paths to pursue creative writing and poetry. His journey as a photographer was further shaped by influential mentors such as John Wood, who encouraged him to explore photography beyond mere documentation. This led Lyons to organize significant photographic exhibitions and establish the Visual Studies Workshop, fostering dialogue between contemporary art and societal issues.

In his later work, Lyons focuses on urban wall writings and posters, which he interprets as temporal expressions of social messages. He arranges these images into sequences that reflect themes like time and societal commentary across multiple books over several decades. Through this method, Lyons aims to create a layered narrative that invites viewers to revisit and reinterpret the imagery repeatedly, uncovering deeper meanings each time.
