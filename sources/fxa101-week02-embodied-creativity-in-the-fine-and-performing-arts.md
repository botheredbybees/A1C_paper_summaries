## Embodied creativity in the fine and performing arts

The article explores the concept of embodied creativity within the fine and performing arts, arguing that it should be recognized as a significant area of study alongside cognitive approaches to creativity. It critiques traditional views in academia that prioritize cognition over embodiment, highlighting how creative processes are often grounded in physical experiences and interactions with materials and environments. The author introduces 4E (Ecological, Embodied, Extended, Enactive) theory to frame the discussion on embodied creativity, emphasizing its importance for understanding creative practices that involve the body. The paper also discusses how the fine arts and performing arts provide ideal contexts for studying embodied creativity due to their reliance on physical engagement. Through a literature review, Griffith outlines the challenges of quantitatively measuring embodied creativity while advocating for a more nuanced approach that values subjective experiences and political dimensions of embodiment. This perspective is crucial for moving towards decoloniality in creativity studies.
