## How WCAG benefits everyone: A focus on neurodiversity and accessibility

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are designed to improve digital experiences for users with visible disabilities, but they also benefit individuals who are neurodivergent. Neurodiversity encompasses a broad range of cognitive differences such as autism, dyslexia, and ADHD, which can affect how these individuals interact with the web. The article highlights common barriers that neurodivergent people face online, including sensory overload from bright colors or flashing animations, difficulty reading large blocks of text or complex fonts, time constraints due to conditions like ADHD, and unpredictable navigation changes. To address these issues, WCAG provides specific success criteria such as allowing users to control auto-playing audio, providing adjustable time limits, giving options to pause moving content, ensuring consistent navigation layouts, and using clear labels for buttons and icons. The guidelines ultimately benefit all users by improving readability, simplifying navigation, and offering flexible timers. Designers are encouraged to simplify website layouts, use high-contrast text, provide text alternatives for audio content, test websites with neurodivergent users, and educate their teams about WCAG and the importance of designing inclusively. By adhering to these guidelines, designers can create digital experiences that empower everyone to participate fully in the online world.
