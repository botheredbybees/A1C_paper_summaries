## Music and spatial task performance

This article reports on a study investigating the relationship between listening to music and performance in spatial reasoning tasks. The research found that college students performed better in abstract/spatial reasoning tests after listening to Mozart's sonata for two pianos compared to relaxation instructions or silence conditions. Thirty-six participants were tested using the Stanford-Binet intelligence scale, revealing a significant difference in standard age scores (SAS) between the music condition and the other two conditions. The mean SAS was 57.56 for the music condition, while it was 54.61 for relaxation instructions and 54.00 for silence. The results indicate that listening to Mozart's music can temporarily enhance spatial reasoning abilities without affecting physiological arousal levels such as pulse rates. This study contributes to understanding the potential cognitive benefits of musical engagement in enhancing specific aspects of human intelligence, particularly relevant to the creative arts and health disciplines.
