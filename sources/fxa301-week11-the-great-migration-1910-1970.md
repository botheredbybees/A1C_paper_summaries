## The Great Migration (1910-1970)

The document provides an overview of the Great Migration, a significant demographic shift in American history that occurred between 1910 and 1970. Approximately six million African Americans moved from southern states to northern, midwestern, and western regions seeking better economic opportunities, educational advancements, and freedom from racial violence and Jim Crow laws. The migration can be divided into two phases: the first phase (1910-1940) was fueled by industrial labor demands during World War I and led to a mass movement of African Americans to cities like New York, Chicago, Detroit, and Pittsburgh. This period also witnessed significant racial tensions, including the Red Summer of 1919, when white communities protested against demographic changes brought about by this migration. The second phase (during and after World War II) saw further expansion in defense industry jobs, encouraging African Americans to move even further westward into cities such as Los Angeles and San Francisco. Despite these moves towards better economic prospects, migrants faced housing discrimination through restrictive covenants and redlining policies, which exacerbated existing racial disparities in wealth within the United States.
