## Musical Imagery Involves Wernicke’s Area in Bilateral and Anti-Correlated Network Interactions in Musicians

This study explores the neural mechanisms underlying musical imagery, defined as imagining music without hearing it. The research involves musicians who continuously imagine an 8-minute symphony while watching a silent movie that provides visual cues to guide their mental imagery process during functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI). The findings reveal overlapping activations between musical imagery and perception in the anterolateral belt and Wernicke's area of the brain. However, while musical perception shows network interactions within the auditory system, musical imagery involves more complex networks that positively correlate with the dorsal attention network and motor-control network but negatively correlate with the default-mode network. This research underscores the role of bilateral and anti-correlated network interactions in forming vivid musical imagery, challenging traditional views on segregated music versus language processing.
