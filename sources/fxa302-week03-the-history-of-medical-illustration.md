## The history of medical illustration

Gabriel Donald’s article, 'The history of medical illustration,' provides a comprehensive overview of the evolution of medical illustrations from ancient civilizations to the Renaissance period. The author highlights early Egyptian and Greek influences, noting that while detailed surgical texts existed in Egypt around 1700 BC, these were devoid of illustrations. Greek philosopher Aristotle is credited with using drawings in his teachings, though significant advancements did not occur until Alexandrian anatomists produced schematic figures in the late 4th Century BC.

The article details how the Roman Empire’s practical approach to science contrasted with earlier theoretical investigations, particularly through the work of Galen in the 2nd Century AD. Following this period, medical illustrations became rare during the Dark Ages but gradually re-emerged in the 11th century as ecclesiastical art influenced their style.

The Renaissance marked a pivotal era for medical illustration with the advent of printing technology and human dissection becoming officially sanctioned by the Church. Leonardo da Vinci’s contributions are emphasized, showcasing his pioneering use of detailed anatomical drawings to enhance understanding of the human body. These developments underscored the importance of visual representation in advancing medical knowledge and practice.
