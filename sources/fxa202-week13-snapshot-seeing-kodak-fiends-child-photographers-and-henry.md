## Snapshot Seeing: Kodak Fiends, Child Photographers, and Henry James's What Maisie Knew

In 'Snapshot Seeing,' Angela Berkley explores the visual and narrative techniques used in Henry James's novel, 'What Maisie Knew.' The article argues that James employs a photographic approach to perception, particularly through the eyes of young Maisie. While naturalist authors like Frank Norris depict child observers as inarticulate and naive, James uses these children to challenge conventional notions of understanding and knowledge. Berkley highlights how the snapshot photography popularized by Eastman Kodak during this period influenced James's writing style, emphasizing quick and enduring visual captures that reflect deeper truths about human experience. Through this lens, Maisie's fragmented yet insightful observations become a form of material knowledge that allows her to understand and control her circumstances better than those around her.
