## PWDA Language Guide: A guide to language about disability

The PWDA Language Guide is a comprehensive resource written by people with disabilities to educate the public and media on appropriate language use when discussing or reporting on individuals with disabilities. It emphasizes that the way people are described can shape societal perceptions, influence how disabled individuals are treated, and impact their self-perception and interactions within society. The guide highlights the negative implications of using terms like 'victim' or 'sufferer,' which imply pity and perpetuate harmful stereotypes. Instead, it promotes respectful language that acknowledges disability as an aspect of human diversity rather than a deficit.

The document also addresses the importance of the social model of disability over the medical model, stressing that societal barriers rather than impairments themselves are what create limitations for individuals with disabilities. It explains the concept of identity-first versus person-first language and advocates for respecting individual preferences when referring to disabled people. Additionally, it critiques ableist language and media portrayals that sensationalize or sentimentalize disability, aiming to promote fair and dignified representation in all forms of communication.
