## The Role of Music and Cultural Activities in Promoting Health and Wellbeing Across Different Age Groups

This chapter discusses the impact of music and cultural activities on health and wellbeing across different age groups. It begins with a study by Lindblad et al. (2007) demonstrating that extra music lessons in schools can improve social cohesion among children, potentially reducing stress levels as indicated by decreased salivary cortisol concentrations. Another study by Theorell et al. (2013) explores the relationship between workplace cultural activities and employee mental health over two years, finding a positive correlation between cultural activity frequency and reduced emotional exhaustion. Hartzell and Theorell's 2007 research highlights the importance of interactive experiences in enhancing vitality during work hours. Theorell et al.'s (2009) study further confirms that higher levels of engagement in cultural activities are associated with better sleep quality and lower plasma fibrinogen levels, indicative of reduced biological stress. A Finnish study by Vaag et al. (2013) examines the participation of healthcare professionals in choir singing as a form of cultural activity, noting significant improvements in work engagement and health among participants. Additionally, Romanowska et al.'s 2011-2013 research shows that arts-based interventions can improve managerial competence, leading to better mental health outcomes for employees. Finally, Bygren et al. (2009) report on cultural activities enhancing various aspects of self-reported health among workers in a municipality, while Arnetz et al.'s 1983 and Wikström's 1994 studies provide experimental evidence that fine art interventions can improve emotional balance and physical health in older women.
