## Equality Act and Disability Discrimination

The Equality Act serves as a regulatory framework aimed at preventing discrimination against individuals based on various protected characteristics including disabilities. The effectiveness of this act in addressing disability discrimination is examined through contrasting perspectives offered by the social model and medical model of disability. According to sources, the social model emphasizes societal barriers that disable people rather than individual impairments; it views disability as a form of oppression rooted in discriminatory practices and negative attitudes. Conversely, the medical model perceives disability as an impairment or health condition needing treatment. In this context, the Equality Act can be seen through two lenses: one aligning with the social model's transformative goals by addressing systemic discrimination, while another sees it merely correcting individual instances of unfairness aligned more closely to a medical framework. Sources indicate that true progress in reducing disability discrimination requires moving beyond just legal remedies and tackling societal attitudes and structures.

## Key Findings

- The Equality Act aims at preventing discrimination based on disabilities but its effectiveness depends on the underlying model used by society (social or medical).
- A social model approach to disability focuses on eliminating systemic barriers that disable individuals, whereas a medical model emphasizes treating health conditions associated with disability.
- Legal frameworks like the Equality Act are necessary but may not suffice in transforming societal attitudes towards disability without an accompanying shift towards more inclusive practices and policies.

## Open Questions

- How can legal measures such as those outlined in the Equality Act be complemented by social changes to effectively combat disability discrimination?
