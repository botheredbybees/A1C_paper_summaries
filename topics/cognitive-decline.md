## Cognitive Decline

This synthesis examines interventions targeting cognitive decline, focusing on antidiabetic drugs as potential therapeutic agents for Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and AD-like dementia. The review by fxa100-week01-effect-of-antidiabetic-drugs-in-alzheimers-disease-a highlights the disruptions in brain insulin signaling common to both type 2 diabetes mellitus and AD, suggesting a role for antidiabetic medications like GLP1 receptor agonists (e.g., exenatide), thiazolidinediones (e.g., pioglitazone), biguanides (e.g., metformin), among others. These drugs show promising effects in preclinical models concerning tau pathology, neuronal health, oxidative stress, neuroinflammation, vascular alterations, brain glucose metabolism, and cognitive function. In contrast, the transcript from fxa100-week01-transcript-alzheimers-society provides a broader understanding of dementia as a set of symptoms caused by neurodegenerative diseases like AD, emphasizing the variability in symptom presentation based on the area of the brain affected by cell death. While there is agreement that interventions targeting specific pathways disrupted in cognitive decline could offer therapeutic benefits, the need for further clinical research to optimize these treatments is evident.

## Key Findings

- Disruptions in brain insulin signaling are common between type 2 diabetes mellitus and Alzheimer’s disease.
- Antidiabetic medications show promising effects on tau pathology and neuronal health in preclinical models of AD.
- Dementia symptoms vary based on the region of the brain affected by cell death.

## Open Questions

- How can antidiabetic drugs be optimized to effectively treat cognitive decline in clinical settings?
