## Constructivism

Constructivism, as explored in the dialogue with Jean Piaget's theories of development, centers on the idea that individuals actively construct their own understanding and knowledge of the world through experience and reflection. This theory diverges from traditional views by emphasizing the role of active construction over passive absorption of information. The notion of stage theory within this framework suggests distinct phases of cognitive growth, each with its unique characteristics. However, there is a nuanced tension between constructivism's focus on individual conceptual change and the broader implications for educational practices and societal understanding. While Piaget's work laid foundational ideas about how children learn through interaction with their environment, later interpretations and applications have highlighted both strengths and limitations of his stage theory approach.

## Key Findings

- Constructivism emphasizes active construction of knowledge by individuals through experience and reflection.
- Piaget's stage theory describes distinct phases in cognitive development characterized by specific cognitive abilities.
- There is a significant focus on conceptual change within constructivist theory.
