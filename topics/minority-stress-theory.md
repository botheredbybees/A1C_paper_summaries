## Minority Stress Theory

The Minority Stress Theory highlights the unique challenges faced by members of stigmatized groups like LGBT individuals. Both sources emphasize that minority stress impacts mental health through both distal and proximal stressors, with distal factors such as societal stigma playing a role in creating an environment where LGBT youth feel marginalized, while proximal stressors include bullying, violence, family rejection, and internalized homonegativity. Source 1 underscores the psychological impact of living with constant fear and anxiety about social discrimination, whereas Source 2 provides evidence that social isolation and lack of connectedness further exacerbate these mental health issues in LGBT youth. The two sources together show a comprehensive picture where both macro-level societal attitudes and micro-level personal experiences contribute to elevated levels of stress and poorer mental health outcomes for this population.

## Key Findings

- Minority Stress Theory explains the unique challenges faced by members of stigmatized groups, particularly LGBT individuals.
- Distal stressors, such as societal stigma, create an environment where LGBT youth are marginalized and experience fear and anxiety about social discrimination.
- Proximal stressors like bullying, violence, family rejection, and internalized homonegativity directly impact the mental health of LGBT youth.

## Open Questions

- How do interventions aimed at reducing minority stress address both distal societal factors and proximal personal experiences?
