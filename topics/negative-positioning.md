## Negative Positioning of People Diagnosed with Dementia

The concept of negative positioning refers to the ways in which society constructs and perceives individuals diagnosed with dementia, often leading to a diminished view of their capabilities and humanity. This social constructionist theory posits that people with dementia are frequently subjected to diagnostic overshadowing, where symptoms or complaints are attributed solely to their condition rather than being evaluated on their own merit. Across sources, there is agreement about the societal tendency to overlook or understate the experiences and perspectives of those living with dementia, which can lead to inadequate care and a lack of recognition for individual needs beyond the diagnosis.

## Key Findings

- Social constructionist theory highlights how individuals diagnosed with dementia are often negatively positioned in society.
- Diagnostic overshadowing is a common phenomenon where other symptoms or complaints are disregarded due to the prevailing view that they arise from the diagnosis rather than being separate issues.
- The social perception of people living with dementia can significantly impact their access to and quality of care.

## Open Questions

- How do we address and change societal perceptions to ensure more equitable treatment and support for individuals diagnosed with dementia?
