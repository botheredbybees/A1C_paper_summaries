## Classical Music and Tonal Hierarchy

This topic explores the theoretical underpinnings of classical music through the lens of tonal hierarchy as revealed by various musical compositions and research methodologies. Source one focuses on Beethoven's Symphony No. 9, specifically its fourth movement 'Ode to Joy,' highlighting the profound impact this composition has had in shaping perceptions of classical music within a Western context. This movement is celebrated for its innovative use of choral elements in a symphonic setting, which also contributes to discussions about tonal structure and thematic development.

In contrast, Source two delves into the probe tone paradigm developed by Krumhansl and Kessler, focusing on how this method can be applied to understand tonality not only in classical music but also across different musical genres such as rock. This source provides insights into how the tonal hierarchy might vary between genres and raises questions about the applicability of traditional theories of tonality when examining diverse musical forms.

The sources collectively highlight a tension between the established norms of classical music theory and the evolving understanding of tonality within broader musical contexts, suggesting that while classical music maintains certain standards for tonal organization, other musical styles may challenge these conventions.

## Key Findings

- Beethoven's Symphony No. 9 demonstrates significant advancements in thematic development and choral integration within a symphonic framework.
- Krumhansl and Kessler's probe tone paradigm offers a robust method for investigating tonal hierarchy across various musical genres.

## Open Questions

- How do variations in tonal hierarchy between classical music and other genres affect our understanding of universal principles versus genre-specific rules governing musical composition?
