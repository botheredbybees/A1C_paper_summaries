## Vascular Cognitive Impairment Following Stroke

Both sources highlight the progression of vascular cognitive impairment (VCI) to vascular dementia following stroke or small vessel disease. They emphasize that VCI is a condition stemming from compromised brain blood vessels leading to oxygen and nutrient deprivation in brain cells, which can manifest before symptoms appear. The severe stage of this condition is recognized as vascular dementia, often co-occurring with other types like Alzheimer's disease or Lewy body disease to form mixed dementia. Symptoms are varied and depend on the specific brain areas affected but commonly include cognitive decline, speech difficulties, movement changes, behavioral disturbances, and mood disorders.

## Key Findings

- Vascular Cognitive Impairment (VCI) is a precursor condition leading to vascular dementia.
- Mixed dementia occurs when vascular dementia coexists with other forms of dementia such as Alzheimer's or Lewy body disease.
- Symptoms of VCI and resulting vascular dementia vary widely based on the specific brain areas affected but often include cognitive decline, speech difficulties, movement changes, behavioral disturbances, and mood disorders.
