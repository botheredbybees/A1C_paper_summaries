## Patient-Centered Approach in Dementia Care

The patient-centered approach emerges as a critical intervention within dementia care that challenges the conventional, often stereotypically negative positioning of individuals diagnosed with dementia. Source one highlights how the social constructionist theory contributes to understanding 'diagnostic overshadowing', where medical conditions are prioritized over individual needs and preferences. This leads to practices that dehumanize patients by emphasizing their deficits rather than their capabilities and desires. In contrast, Source two introduces a more practical application of the patient-centered approach within institutional settings through an ethnography of care routines in acute hospital wards. It reveals how staff and patients engage in 'routines of resistance', indicating a tacit acknowledgment that current care practices are inadequate for providing person-centered care. While both sources advocate for more empathetic and individualized care, they also expose the challenges in implementing patient-centered approaches due to entrenched institutional practices and societal stereotypes.

## Key Findings

- Patient-centered dementia care requires moving beyond stereotypical views of individuals with dementia.
- Institutional routines often hinder the implementation of truly patient-centered interventions.
- Negative positioning of people diagnosed with dementia leads to diagnostic overshadowing, prioritizing medical conditions over individual needs.

## Open Questions

- How can institutional barriers be effectively overcome to better implement a patient-centered approach in dementia care?
