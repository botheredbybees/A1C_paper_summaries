## Disaster Resilience Through Children's Outdoor Play

The exploration of disaster resilience through the lens of children's outdoor play in both natural and human-induced disaster contexts reveals significant insights into how physical environments can support psychological recovery and community building. The concept of 'found spaces' versus 'constructed spaces' emphasizes the importance of adaptable, flexible areas that can be readily repurposed for play after a disaster. These found spaces offer immediate benefits by providing safe and familiar grounds for children to engage in activities crucial for their emotional well-being and social development during recovery phases. On the other hand, constructed spaces highlight the need for forward-thinking urban planning that incorporates resilience into design principles from the outset. Despite these positive outcomes, there are tensions around the sustainability of found spaces versus the permanence offered by designed infrastructure, suggesting a balance between immediate adaptability and long-term preparedness is necessary.

## Key Findings

- Found spaces offer rapid reclamation of areas for children's outdoor play following disasters, providing psychological benefits.
- Constructed spaces emphasize resilience through design principles that anticipate future needs, enhancing long-term recovery efforts.
- Children's engagement in outdoor activities plays a crucial role in disaster resilience by fostering emotional and social development.

## Open Questions

- How can urban planning effectively balance the immediate adaptability of found spaces with the permanence required for resilient constructed spaces?
