## Brain Health and Cognitive Decline

The sources collectively highlight the multifactorial nature of brain health and cognitive decline. Vascular dementia caused by small vessel disease (Source 1) and Alzheimer's disease characterized by beta-amyloid plaques and tau proteins (Source 4) represent two primary etiologies. Both conditions lead to significant cognitive impairment, although they differ in their pathophysiology and management strategies. Modifiable risk factors for cognitive decline include lifestyle choices such as diet, physical activity, social engagement, and sleep quality (Source 2). Additionally, visceral adipose tissue plays a crucial role in regulating brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) levels through chemokine ligand CX3CL1 (Source 3), which is essential for maintaining cognitive functions. These findings suggest that interventions targeting lifestyle modifications and understanding the biological mechanisms of BDNF regulation may offer promising strategies to mitigate cognitive decline.

## Key Findings

- Vascular dementia and Alzheimer's disease are two major forms of cognitive decline with distinct pathologies but overlapping symptoms.
- Modifiable risk factors such as diet, exercise, social engagement, and sleep quality significantly influence the development of cognitive decline.
- Visceral adipose tissue impacts cognitive functions through regulation of BDNF via CX3CL1.
