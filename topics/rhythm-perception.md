## Rhythm Perception During Development

The exploration of rhythm perception during development highlights the critical role of sensorimotor synchronization (SMS) and spontaneous motor tempo range from infancy through early childhood. Source 1 emphasizes how infants develop rhythmic abilities even before birth, indicating a natural predisposition towards certain tempos that align with typical heart rates and breathing patterns. This intrinsic capability suggests an evolutionary advantage for survival and bonding between caregivers and infants. In contrast, Source 2 discusses the broader context of rhythm perception within brain function studies, particularly focusing on how music listening involves not just auditory but also motor system engagement. The SIMPHONY project referenced in this source aims to bridge gaps by integrating insights from both cognitive neuroscience and developmental psychology to understand how early experiences with rhythmic stimuli shape later musical behaviors and cognitive functions.

## Key Findings

- Infants have a natural predisposition towards sensorimotor synchronization that aligns with physiological tempos such as heart rate and breathing patterns.
- Rhythm perception is deeply intertwined with motor system engagement, not just auditory processing.
- Early rhythmic experiences may influence later cognitive functions and musical behaviors.

## Open Questions

- How do cultural differences in early exposure to rhythm and music impact the development of sensorimotor synchronization?
