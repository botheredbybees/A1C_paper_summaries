## Medical Model Of Disability

The concept of the Medical Model of Disability emerges prominently across sources, contrasting sharply with the Social Model of Disability. Source 1 offers an overview highlighting how the medical model frames disability as a personal tragedy stemming from individual impairments and deficiencies, emphasizing treatment and cure. In contrast, it suggests that social constructs perpetuate disabling conditions by failing to accommodate differences, thus proposing the need for societal changes rather than individual treatments. Sources 2 and 3 delve into more specific contexts where this model's influence is apparent: Source 2 discusses early intervention strategies within autism spectrum disorders, suggesting a reliance on medical definitions of disability that prioritize treatment over social inclusion. Meanwhile, Source 3 critically examines unconscious biases embedded in the medical model within legal frameworks like the Equality Act, arguing for transformative thinking through adopting the Social Model which recognizes exclusion as a disabling factor rather than individual impairment.

## Key Findings

- The Medical Model of Disability is characterized by its focus on deficits and deficiencies at an individual level, contrasting with societal constructs addressed by the Social Model.
- Unconscious biases in legal frameworks like the Equality Act highlight systemic issues perpetuated by the medical model's narrow view of disability.
- Early intervention strategies for autism spectrum disorders often rely on a medical definition of disability which may overlook broader social inclusion and accommodation needs.

## Open Questions

- How can legal and policy frameworks better incorporate principles from the Social Model to challenge and transform unconscious biases embedded in the Medical Model?
