## Gender Disparities in Mental Health

The National Study of Mental Health and Wellbeing from 2020-2022 highlights significant differences in the lifetime and current prevalences of mental health conditions between genders. Across various studies, women consistently report higher rates of anxiety disorders, depression, and eating disorders compared to men. Conversely, men exhibit greater prevalence of substance use disorders. These disparities are exacerbated by social constructs such as gender roles and societal expectations that influence help-seeking behavior and the manifestation of symptoms differently in males and females. However, a notable tension exists regarding the underreporting of mental health issues among certain male populations due to stigma and reluctance to seek treatment. Despite these findings, there remains a gap in understanding how early intervention strategies can mitigate gender-specific risk factors.

## Key Findings

- Women report higher lifetime prevalence rates for anxiety disorders compared to men.
- Men exhibit greater current prevalence of substance use disorders when contrasted with women's data.
- Societal expectations and gender roles significantly influence the manifestation and reporting of mental health issues.

## Open Questions

- How can early intervention strategies effectively address gender-specific risk factors for mental health conditions?
