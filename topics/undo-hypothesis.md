## Undo Hypothesis and Positive Emotions

The concept of the undo hypothesis is a critical component in understanding how positive emotions can mitigate negative effects from stressful events. This hypothesis suggests that positive emotions can 'undo' or reverse the physiological impacts of stress by promoting faster recovery from negative affect states, as discussed across all sources. The broaden-and-build theory, mentioned in each source, underpins this idea by proposing that positive emotional experiences have broadening effects on cognition and action that foster long-term resilience and well-being. Additionally, concepts such as hedonia (the experience of pleasure) from Source 1 contribute to a fuller understanding of subjective well-being. Sources also introduce the stress buffering effect, where positive emotions act as buffers against stressful life events. Together, these sources provide a robust framework for how positive emotions can serve as protective factors in psychological resilience and overall health.

## Key Findings

- Positive emotions can mitigate negative physiological effects of stress through rapid recovery from negative affect states (undo hypothesis).
- The broaden-and-build theory explains the mechanism by which positive emotions promote long-term well-being and resilience.
- Hedonia, or pleasure-seeking behavior, contributes to subjective well-being as part of a broader model of mental health.
