## Early Brain Overgrowth Hypothesis

The Early Brain Overgrowth Hypothesis proposes that autism is characterized by an initial period of excessive brain growth in infancy which may later 'normalize' as the individual ages. However, evidence from a recent study challenges this normalization prediction, suggesting that there are inconsistencies between what is predicted and observed in terms of neurodevelopmental trajectories in individuals with autism spectrum disorder (ASD). The hypothesis implies a direct link between early developmental stages and ASD symptomatology but faces criticism for oversimplifying the complex nature of brain development. This critique emphasizes the need to reassess assumptions about 'normalization' and underscores the importance of understanding diverse pathways within neurodevelopmental disorders.

## Key Findings

- The Early Brain Overgrowth Hypothesis posits an initial phase of excessive brain growth in children with autism spectrum disorder (ASD), which is then followed by a period of normalization.
- Recent studies have provided evidence that disputes the prediction of normalization, indicating divergent developmental trajectories beyond infancy.
- IQ-by-diagnosis interaction plays a significant role in understanding neurodevelopmental outcomes and challenges the hypothesis's predictive accuracy.

## Open Questions
