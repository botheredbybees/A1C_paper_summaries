## Humourism

The concept of humourism is central to understanding the historical approach to health and disease in ancient Greek medicine. This theory proposed that four bodily fluids—blood, phlegm, yellow bile (choler), and black bile (melancholy)—dictated a person's temperament and overall well-being. Source 1 introduces humourism as part of a broader context involving arts like music, which was believed to influence the balance of these humours through its resonance with nature. In Source 2, humourism is placed within a framework that includes catharsis and ethos, highlighting the interconnectedness of emotional states and physical health in ancient Greek thought. The theory of humourism thus served not only as an explanation for medical conditions but also as a basis for therapeutic interventions such as music therapy to restore balance among the humours.

## Key Findings

- Humourism was a foundational theory in ancient Greek medicine explaining health through the balance of four bodily fluids.
- The arts, including music, were considered instrumental in restoring balance within the body according to humourism.
- Catharsis and ethos are additional components of healing that complemented humourism's medical model.

## Open Questions

- How did the ancient Greeks reconcile humourism with other forms of treatment not directly related to balancing bodily fluids?
