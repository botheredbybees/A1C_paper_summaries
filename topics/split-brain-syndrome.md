## Split-Brain Syndrome

Split-brain syndrome arises when the corpus callosum is severed to treat severe epilepsy. Contrary to popular belief, this does not mean each hemisphere operates independently as a separate 'brain' or that people are left-brained or right-brained. Research using fMRI has debunked such myths and shown that while certain functions like language may predominantly reside in one hemisphere, both hemispheres still need to communicate effectively for optimal cognitive function. The syndrome primarily affects the integration of information between hemispheres but does not significantly impair overall brain function.

## Key Findings

- Split-brain syndrome results from surgical severing of the corpus callosum and is used to treat severe epilepsy.
- The notion that each hemisphere functions as a separate 'brain' or people are dominantly left-brained or right-brained is a myth.
- fMRI research has contributed significantly to understanding brain lateralization but also clarified misconceptions about split-brain syndrome.
