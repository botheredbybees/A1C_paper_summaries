## Music Evoked Emotion Response Theory

The theory of Music Evoked Emotion Response (MEER) posits that individuals can experience profound emotional reactions when exposed to music, particularly in therapeutic contexts such as dementia care. Across multiple sources, MEER is discussed within the framework of how musical engagement stimulates cognitive processes and evokes affective states in patients with advanced dementia. The theory underscores the role of familiar tunes in eliciting positive emotions and nostalgia, thereby enhancing quality of life for individuals suffering from memory decline. However, some sources suggest that while music therapy offers promising benefits, there is a lack of standardized protocols and empirical evidence to fully support its efficacy universally across all patient populations. Additionally, MEER raises questions about the mechanisms through which music influences emotional and cognitive functions in dementia patients, highlighting the need for further investigation into personalized approaches based on individual response variability.

## Key Findings

- Music therapy enhances quality of life by evoking positive emotions and stimulating cognition in individuals with advanced dementia.
- Familiar music is particularly effective in triggering nostalgic responses and emotional engagement.
- There are gaps in standardized protocols for implementing MEER across different populations.

## Open Questions

- What are the underlying neurological mechanisms through which music influences emotion and cognitive function in dementia patients?
