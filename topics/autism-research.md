## Autism Spectrum Disorders

The exploration of autism spectrum disorders (ASD) reveals a complex interplay between neurodiversity theory and biological underpinnings. Source 1 introduces 'neurodiversity' as a framework that views ASD not as a disorder but as a distinct way of being, emphasizing cultural practices such as stimming and questioning power dynamics in therapy settings. This perspective challenges traditional medical models by advocating for social justice and inclusivity. In contrast, Source 2 delves into the biological mechanisms underlying ASD, highlighting mitochondrial dysfunction, lipid-related pathophysiology, and dysfunctional circuitry mechanisms. While these sources differ in their approach—Source 1 focuses on societal and cultural factors while Source 2 emphasizes neurological and physiological elements—they both contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of ASD, suggesting that effective approaches might need to integrate both biological treatments and social support.

## Key Findings

- Neurodiversity theory challenges traditional medical models by advocating for the recognition of autism as a distinct way of being rather than a disorder.
- Biological research into ASD highlights specific physiological mechanisms such as mitochondrial dysfunction and lipid-related pathophysiology that contribute to neurological differences in individuals with ASD.
- There is a need for integrating both biological interventions and social support systems in addressing the needs of individuals on the autism spectrum.

## Open Questions

- How can neurodiversity theory be effectively integrated into clinical practice to improve outcomes for individuals with ASD?
