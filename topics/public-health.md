## Creative Arts in Public Health

The integration of creative arts into public health interventions presents a multifaceted approach to addressing societal well-being and health issues. Sources emphasize the importance of biopsychosocial models, which consider biological, psychological, and social factors (Haldane-D), alongside salutogenic approaches that focus on promoting health rather than preventing illness or treating disease (Oxford Textbook). These frameworks underpin various creative arts initiatives aimed at enhancing emotional and cognitive resources through music therapy, visual arts, and expressive writing. Additionally, community engagement in cultural development projects highlights the potential of art to foster social change (Creative Arts Initiatives for Cultural Development). Museums, in particular, are noted as effective settings for health and well-being interventions, addressing issues like social isolation and mental health support among dementia patients (Museums for Health and Wellbeing). The effectiveness of these interventions is further validated through specific examples such as the Big Noise Raploch program, which shows positive educational outcomes and employment rates post-schooling. However, there are also studies focusing on more traditional public health concerns like fall prevention in older adults through social dancing (Social Dancing to Reduce Falls), indicating that creative arts can be integrated into a wide range of public health strategies.

## Key Findings

- Creative arts interventions enhance emotional and cognitive resources among participants.
- Community engagement through cultural development projects fosters social change.
- Museums serve as effective settings for health and well-being, addressing mental health and reducing social isolation.
- Music therapy and other expressive art forms contribute positively to public health outcomes.
