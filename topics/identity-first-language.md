## Identity-First Language

The use of language when referring to people with disabilities is a critical aspect that influences how these individuals perceive themselves and are perceived by society. The PWDA (People With Disability Australia) Language Guide advocates for the use of identity-first language over person-first language, suggesting it as more empowering and reflective of personal choice rather than medical diagnosis. Identity-first language involves placing the identifier of disability before the noun, such as 'disabled person,' emphasizing that disability is an integral part of a person's identity. The guide highlights the importance of using respectful terminology by avoiding ableist language which can be derogatory or dehumanizing towards individuals with disabilities. There are varying opinions on whether to use identity-first versus person-first language, but the guiding principle should be to respect individual preferences and the evolving norms within communities of people with disabilities.

## Key Findings

- Identity-first language emphasizes disability as an integral part of a person's identity rather than a separate attribute.
- Respectful language use is crucial in how individuals perceive themselves and are perceived by society.
- Ableist language should be avoided to prevent derogatory or dehumanizing descriptions.
