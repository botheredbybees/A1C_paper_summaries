## Disability Rights and Inclusion

The documents explore the intersection between disability rights theory and practical applications in arts projects. The first document emphasizes inclusiveness in arts planning under a social model of disability, which sees disability as a result of societal barriers rather than individual pathology. This approach advocates for removing such obstacles to ensure full participation of people with disabilities in cultural activities. However, Source 2 critiques the social model by introducing Hannah Arendt's concept of power, suggesting that it fails to address systemic issues deeply enough and needs broader transformative change beyond mere inclusion. Both sources highlight the ongoing debate about how best to empower individuals with disabilities within society.

## Key Findings

- The social model of disability emphasizes societal barriers as the cause of disablement rather than individual impairment.
- There is a need for arts projects to be inclusive, reflecting principles of disability rights and the social model.
- Critiques suggest that the social model may not fully address systemic issues or transformative change needed in society.

## Open Questions

- How can the limitations of the social model identified by critiques be addressed while maintaining its core benefits?
