## Maslow's Hierarchy Of Needs

The concept of Maslow's hierarchy of needs is central to understanding human motivation and well-being. It posits that individuals are motivated by a series of basic needs, which must be satisfied in a hierarchical order for psychological health and self-actualization. In the context of dementia care, the application of this theory highlights its importance in addressing physiological, safety, love/belonging, esteem, and self-actualization needs of patients. This is often integrated with Kitwood’s person-centered care model to emphasize the holistic and relational aspects of care provision. However, while Maslow's hierarchy provides a foundational framework for understanding basic human needs, it may not sufficiently address the complex interplay between psychological and physiological factors unique to dementia patients. Thus, its utility in practical care settings is enriched when complemented with other theoretical models.

## Key Findings

- Maslow's hierarchy of needs outlines a sequential fulfillment model critical for individual well-being.
- The theory is applied alongside Kitwood’s person-centered approach to provide comprehensive care in dementia management.
- There are limitations in applying Maslow's hierarchy without considering the unique psychological and physiological challenges faced by individuals with dementia.

## Open Questions

- How can Maslow's hierarchy of needs be effectively integrated into modern healthcare practices for dementia patients, especially when traditional hierarchical approaches may not fully address their complex needs?
