## Stigma Reduction Through Language

The integration of person-first language and non-stigmatizing communication emerges as a core strategy for reducing stigma across various populations, particularly those with dementia or experiencing mental health issues. Both 'A Guide to Dementia-Friendly Language' and 'Dementia Language Guidelines' advocate for the use of terms that emphasize the individual before their condition (e.g., 'person with dementia') over terms that may reduce individuals to a diagnosis alone. Cultural sensitivity is also highlighted as an essential component in both guides, suggesting tailored language approaches to accommodate diverse cultural backgrounds. The 'Recovery Oriented Language Guide' extends these principles to include recovery-promotion and stigma-prevention strategies specifically aimed at mental health service users. Despite similarities in the emphasis on person-first language and cultural sensitivity, there is a nuanced difference: while dementia-focused guidelines focus narrowly on language as a tool for creating supportive environments, the Recovery Oriented Language Guide also emphasizes the role of communication in facilitating recovery and preventing future stigma.

## Key Findings

- Person-first language is recommended to reduce stigma.
- Non-stigmatizing communication includes cultural sensitivity.
- Tailored approaches are necessary for effective language use.

## Open Questions
