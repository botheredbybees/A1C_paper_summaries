## Singing as an Intervention for Dementia and Caregiver Wellbeing

The synthesis of the provided sources highlights singing as a valuable intervention that enhances both physical and mental health, particularly in individuals with dementia and their caregivers. Source 1 focuses on 'Singing for the Brain', a program aimed at improving quality of life among people living with dementia by engaging them in group singing activities. This qualitative study reveals significant benefits such as increased social interaction, cognitive stimulation, emotional support, and overall well-being. Meanwhile, Source 2 presents a broader perspective through its Creative Health Review Report which underscores singing as one form of creative activity that can contribute to holistic health improvements, including mental and physical preventive healthcare approaches. The report suggests policy recommendations to incorporate such arts-based interventions within community health initiatives.

## Key Findings

- Singing in groups provides substantial benefits for individuals with dementia and their caregivers
- Creative activities like singing have potential for enhancing overall quality of life and promoting holistic health improvements
- There is a growing recognition that policy frameworks should support the integration of arts-based interventions into public health strategies

## Open Questions

- What are the long-term impacts of group singing on dementia progression and caregiver stress?
