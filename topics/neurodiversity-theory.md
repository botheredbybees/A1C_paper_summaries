## Neurodiversity Theory

The concept of neurodiversity theory is fundamentally about recognizing the neurological differences within humans as natural variations rather than deficits to be fixed. This perspective emphasizes that autism and other neurodevelopmental conditions should not be viewed through a medical model but rather from a social justice lens, advocating for acceptance and support in society instead of attempting to 'cure' or 'normalize' individuals. The source discusses how this theory challenges traditional power dynamics inherent in therapeutic settings, suggesting that practices such as stimming (self-stimulatory behaviors) should be understood within cultural contexts rather than merely as symptoms of a disorder. However, the application and understanding of neurodiversity vary widely among different communities and professionals.

## Key Findings

- Neurodiversity theory promotes acceptance and respect for neurological differences over attempts at normalization or 'cure'.
- Power dynamics in therapeutic settings can conflict with neurodiversity principles, leading to challenges in fully embracing this perspective.
- Stimming is recognized as a cultural practice within the neurodivergent community rather than merely symptomatic behavior.

## Open Questions

- How can the medical and neurodiversity perspectives be reconciled or integrated for more holistic support of individuals with neurological differences?
