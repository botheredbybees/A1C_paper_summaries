## Art as a Window into the Experience of Alzheimer's Disease

The study of visual art created by individuals with Alzheimer's disease offers unique insights into both the visuoconstructural deficits experienced during dementia and the subjective experience of illness. Source 1 examines specific paintings to identify patterns in visuoconstructive abilities, such as changes in line quality, color use, and spatial organization indicative of cognitive decline associated with Alzheimer's. In contrast, Source 2 focuses on self-portraits that reveal not only technical deficits but also the personal struggle and emotional impact of living with Alzheimer's. Both sources highlight how visual art serves as a medium to bridge the gap between clinical understanding and the subjective reality of dementia, providing a more holistic view of the condition beyond traditional diagnostic criteria.

## Key Findings

- Visual arts can serve as an expressive tool for individuals experiencing Alzheimer's disease, revealing aspects of their cognitive decline not easily captured through medical assessments alone.
- Self-portraits and other forms of personal artwork provide insight into the emotional journey of living with Alzheimer's beyond just physical symptoms.

## Open Questions

- How can art-based observations be integrated more systematically into clinical practice for earlier identification or improved understanding of individual patient experiences in dementia?
