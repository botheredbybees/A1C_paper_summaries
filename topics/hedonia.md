## Hedonia

The concept of hedonia emerges as a significant element in discussions on psychological theories of well-being. Both hedonia and its counterpart, eudaimonia, are central to understanding subjective well-being (SWB) and the broader field of positive psychology. Hedonia is primarily associated with pleasure-seeking behaviors that bring about immediate satisfaction or happiness, whereas eudaimonic approaches focus more on fulfilling personal potential, purpose, and self-actualization. The experience machine thought experiment raises questions about the value of genuine experiences versus fabricated ones that provide similar levels of hedonic satisfaction, challenging the adequacy of hedonia as a sole indicator of well-being. Sources also highlight the 'broaden-and-build' theory and the 'undo hypothesis', suggesting that positive emotions can broaden cognitive perspectives and build enduring personal resources, contributing to long-term SWB beyond mere hedonic pleasure.

## Key Findings

- Hedonia represents immediate pleasure-seeking behavior compared to eudaimonia's focus on purpose-driven fulfillment.
- Subjective well-being theories incorporate both hedonic (pleasure) and eudaimonic (fulfillment) aspects of happiness.
- The experience machine thought experiment questions the value of genuine experiences in achieving true satisfaction.

## Open Questions

- To what extent does the 'broaden-and-build' theory adequately address the role of hedonia versus eudaimonia in psychological well-being?
