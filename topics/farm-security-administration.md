## Farm Security Administration

The Farm Security Administration (FSA) is a governmental intervention established during the Great Depression to address rural poverty and agricultural issues. Central to its narrative is Dorothea Lange's iconic photograph, 'Migrant Mother,' which symbolizes the plight of migrant farm workers. The FSA was instrumental in documenting social conditions through photography and journalism, aiming to elicit public support for federal relief programs. Its work included providing emergency assistance, improving credit facilities, and promoting sound farming practices among other initiatives designed to aid struggling farmers and rural communities. While 'Migrant Mother' captures a poignant moment of hardship, it also underscores the FSA's broader efforts to understand and address the needs of marginalized populations in agricultural settings.

## Key Findings

- The Farm Security Administration was established as part of New Deal programs to combat poverty among farmers during the Great Depression.
- Photography played an essential role in documenting rural life under the FSA, influencing public perception and policy support.
- Dorothea Lange's 'Migrant Mother' epitomizes the human face of agricultural hardship, reflecting broader themes of struggle and resilience.
