## Conceptual Change

The concept of conceptual change is deeply rooted in the theories of Jean Piaget and extends beyond his framework to influence educational psychology. Constructivism, a cornerstone of Piaget's theory, posits that individuals construct their understanding and knowledge of the world through experiences and reflection upon these experiences. This process involves actively constructing new ideas or concepts based on current and past knowledge. The stage theory proposed by Piaget outlines specific stages of cognitive development during which conceptual change can occur. However, while Piaget’s work has been influential, subsequent research in educational psychology suggests that learning and cognitive development are more nuanced than the strict stage progression outlined by Piaget, indicating a need for adaptation and expansion of his original ideas to better explain how individuals move from one conception to another.

## Key Findings

- Constructivism forms a foundational theory of conceptual change, emphasizing active construction of knowledge through experiences and reflection.
- Piaget's stage theory provides a framework for understanding cognitive development stages that involve conceptual change.
- Subsequent research suggests the need for more flexible models of learning beyond Piaget’s strict stage progression.

## Open Questions

- How can constructivist theories be adapted to better account for individual differences in the process and timing of conceptual change?
