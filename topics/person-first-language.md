## Person-First Language

The concept of person-first language is emphasized across sources as a crucial aspect in how individuals with conditions such as dementia and disabilities are addressed. Sources advocate for the use of phrases that place the individual before their condition (e.g., 'person living with dementia' instead of 'demented person') to prioritize the humanity over illness. This approach aims at reducing stigma and promoting dignity, yet sources also recognize exceptions where identity-first language may be preferred by certain individuals or communities based on personal preference or cultural context. Cultural sensitivity is another recurring theme in these guidelines, urging professionals and caregivers to respect diverse perspectives when applying language recommendations. The impact of such language choices on the perception and treatment of those with dementia is highlighted, suggesting that appropriate communication can foster a more inclusive environment and improve interaction quality.

## Key Findings

- Person-first language emphasizes individual dignity over diagnosis
- Cultural sensitivity influences the application of person-first language guidelines
- Language choices impact perceptions about individuals with conditions like dementia

## Open Questions

- What are the best practices for balancing cultural sensitivity and adherence to person-first language principles?
