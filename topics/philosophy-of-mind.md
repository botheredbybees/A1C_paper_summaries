## Philosophy of Mind

The exploration of the philosophy of mind within the context of dementia care highlights diverse theoretical frameworks that challenge traditional views on memory and personhood. Source 1 discusses contrasting causal and constitutive accounts, questioning whether memories are internal or influenced by external factors. This debate suggests a shift towards an externality of mind perspective, where cognitive processes are not solely confined to individual brains but are also shaped by environmental interactions. Meanwhile, Source 2 emphasizes the importance of embodied selfhood in dementia care, focusing on how physical experiences and bodily autonomy contribute significantly to personal identity. The concepts of tacit knowledge and sedimentation of habits underscore that individuals' actions and behaviors are deeply rooted in their physical and social environments, further supporting an external perspective on cognition. Finally, Source 3 advocates for a humanistic approach based on contextual interpretation and social constructivism, proposing that memory and cognitive functioning are heavily influenced by social interactions and cultural contexts. This collective discourse reveals a shift from traditional individualist views of the mind towards more relational and embedded understandings.

## Key Findings

- Memory is increasingly seen as an externally influenced process rather than solely internal
- Embodied selfhood and bodily autonomy are crucial in defining personal identity, particularly for individuals with dementia
- Social interactions and cultural contexts significantly shape cognitive functions such as memory

## Open Questions

- How can the integration of externalist views on mind impact clinical practices in dementia care?
