## Confounding Factors in Health Interventions

The integration of findings from sources examining arts-based interventions and risk-of-bias assessment tools reveals significant challenges in methodological rigor within health intervention studies. Both sources highlight the critical role of confounding factors, which can undermine causal inference in both randomized and non-randomized designs. Source one discusses the importance of rigorous randomization to mitigate selection bias and control for confounders. However, it also points out that definitions of 'art' might vary widely across different contexts, potentially introducing additional variability that complicates the establishment of a clear cause-effect relationship. Meanwhile, source two introduces ROBINS-I V2, a tool specifically designed to assess risk of bias in non-randomized studies, emphasizing the need for a target trial comparison and the importance of per-protocol effect analyses to capture real-world conditions more accurately. Together, these sources underscore the necessity for robust methodological frameworks that account for confounding variables while also acknowledging the practical limitations inherent in randomized controlled trials.

## Key Findings

- Randomization is crucial for mitigating selection bias and controlling for confounding factors.
- Definitions of key intervention elements (like 'art') can introduce variability, complicating causal inference.
- Risk-of-bias assessment tools are essential for evaluating non-randomized studies.

## Open Questions

- How do varying definitions of core interventions impact the generalizability and reproducibility of health intervention studies?
