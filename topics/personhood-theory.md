## Personhood in Dementia Care

The concept of personhood within dementia care has emerged as a critical theoretical framework that seeks to affirm and maintain the dignity and identity of individuals despite their cognitive decline. Source 2 highlights the 'situated embodied agent' (SEA) theory, which posits that even in advanced stages of dementia, individuals retain agency through their physical presence and interactions with others, thus preserving a sense of personhood. This perspective challenges traditional views that see dementia patients as passive recipients of care, instead emphasizing emotional connections and recognition of the whole individual beyond just cognitive function.

Source 1 delves into changes in communication for persons with dementia, noting the importance of non-verbal cues and malignant social psychology. It suggests that while verbal abilities may deteriorate, non-verbal expressions continue to convey a sense of self and identity, reinforcing the SEA theory's emphasis on embodied agency. This source also points towards how care environments can foster or hinder the expression of personhood through their communication practices.

Source 3 elaborates further by introducing key concepts such as 'embodied selfhood' and bodily autonomy, which underpin the maintenance of individual identity even in later stages of dementia. It underscores that personal identity is not solely cognitive but also rooted in physical experiences and interactions over time ('sedimentation of habits'). The source discusses the ethical implications of recognizing personhood in dementia care, advocating for rights and respect based on an understanding of each patient as a unique entity with inherent value.

Overall, these sources collectively underscore the importance of viewing persons with dementia through the lens of their ongoing personal identity rather than just focusing on cognitive decline. They suggest that maintaining connections to physical experiences and emotional relationships is crucial in affirming personhood.

## Key Findings

- The 'situated embodied agent' (SEA) theory emphasizes the role of non-verbal communication and physical presence in preserving personhood for individuals with dementia.
- Maintaining recognition of personal identity through embodied selfhood and bodily autonomy supports ethical care practices that respect rights and dignity.
- Changes in communication patterns do not negate the ability to express individuality; rather, they require adaptation from caregivers.

## Open Questions

- How can healthcare settings be restructured to better support non-verbal forms of personhood expression for individuals with advanced dementia?
