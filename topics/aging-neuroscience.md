## Neurobiological Changes in Aging

This synthesis explores neurobiological changes in aging through the lenses of sundown syndrome and synesthesia. Sundown syndrome highlights disruptions in circadian rhythms due to degeneration of the suprachiasmatic nucleus, which manages daily biological cycles including sleep-wake patterns. In contrast, Meier's work on synesthesia illustrates consistent grapheme-to-color associations that remain stable throughout adult life but may be influenced by aging-related changes. While sundown syndrome points towards age-induced neurodegeneration affecting circadian rhythms, synesthesia suggests that certain neural connections can persist over a lifetime with minor adjustments. This juxtaposition of neurobiological stability and change raises questions about the extent to which various neurological functions are resilient or vulnerable to aging.

## Key Findings

- Circadian rhythm disruptions in dementia highlight age-related degeneration in circadian regulation centers like the suprachiasmatic nucleus.
- Synesthesia demonstrates that certain neural connections can maintain stability across adult life despite cognitive aging.
- Age-related changes in synesthesia suggest a nuanced view of how sensory and perceptual experiences evolve with aging.

## Open Questions

- What are the mechanisms by which certain neurological functions remain stable or change over time, and what factors influence this variability?
