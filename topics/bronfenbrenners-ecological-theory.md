## Bronfenbrenner’s Ecological Theory

Bronfenbrenner's ecological systems theory offers a comprehensive framework to understand human development across various environmental contexts. Unlike Freud's psychoanalytic theory, which emphasizes the unconscious mind and sexual energy as driving forces of behavior, or Piaget’s cognitive theory, focusing on stages of intellectual growth, Bronfenbrenner’s model considers an individual holistically by including multiple layers of interaction with their environment. This includes microsystems (immediate contexts such as family), mesosystems (interconnections between immediate contexts), exosystems (indirect settings that affect the person’s environment but are not directly experienced there), and macrosystems (cultural values and laws). These systems interact dynamically over time, influencing each other and shaping individual development. This framework highlights the importance of considering broader societal influences alongside direct personal experiences in studying developmental psychology.

## Key Findings

- Bronfenbrenner’s ecological theory provides a holistic perspective on human development by incorporating multiple environmental contexts beyond immediate surroundings.
- In contrast to Freud's psychoanalytic and Piaget’s cognitive theories, Bronfenbrenner focuses not only on individual stages but also on how these interact with various systems within society.
- The dynamic interaction between different ecological layers suggests that development is a complex process influenced by reciprocal relationships among personal attributes and environmental factors.
