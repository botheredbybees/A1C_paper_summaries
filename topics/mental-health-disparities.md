## Mental Health Disparities Among Vulnerable Populations

The analysis of mental health disparities among vulnerable populations reveals distinct challenges faced by different groups following traumatic events and in the context of societal stigma. The Great East Japan earthquake led to an increase in mandatory psychiatric emergency hospitalizations, indicating heightened mental distress due to indirect disaster exposure (Source 1). Meanwhile, LGBT youth experience unique stressors stemming from minority stress theory, which encompasses both distal (e.g., discrimination) and proximal (e.g., internalized homonegativity) factors that contribute significantly to their mental health issues (Source 2). Additionally, a preliminary study of an arts-based suicide prevention program for sexual and gender minority youth highlights the critical role of emotional regulation skills in mitigating feelings of thwarted belongingness and depressive symptoms (Source 3), further underscoring the multifaceted nature of mental health disparities faced by marginalized groups. These studies collectively suggest that while emergency interventions are necessary post-disaster, long-term support focused on building resilience through emotional regulation and addressing societal stigma is crucial for improving mental health outcomes.

## Key Findings

- Mandatory psychiatric hospitalizations increased in Tokyo following the Great East Japan earthquake, reflecting indirect exposure to disaster's impact on mental health
- LGBT youth face higher levels of mental distress due to distal stressors such as discrimination and proximal stressors like internalized homonegativity
- Arts-based programs can aid sexual and gender minority youth in developing emotional regulation skills that help reduce feelings of thwarted belongingness

## Open Questions

- What are the long-term mental health impacts on populations affected by indirect disaster exposure, and how can these be mitigated through targeted interventions?
