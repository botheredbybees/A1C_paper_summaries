## Historical Medical Practices

The historical medical practices explored in the sources illustrate a convergence of artistic and scientific paradigms in treatment approaches. The first source highlights the use of color therapy at the Red Cross Russell Lea Nerve Home, where patients suffering from nervous disorders were treated with abstract color theory to alleviate symptoms, reflecting an early form of holistic medicine that combined art's therapeutic effects with medical care. In contrast, the second source delves into ancient Greek medical thought regarding jaundice through the lens of Hippocratic humoral theory, which posited that health and disease result from an imbalance among four bodily fluids or 'humors'. This method reflects a more empirical yet holistic approach to diagnosing and treating conditions based on the prevailing physiological understanding of the time. Both sources underscore the historical shift towards integrating subjective experiences with objective medical knowledge in therapeutic interventions.

## Key Findings

- Historical medical practices often integrated contemporary scientific theories and artistic principles into treatment approaches.
- Color therapy was employed as a method to treat nervous disorders by applying abstract color theory.
- Hippocratic humoral theory provided an early framework for understanding bodily imbalances leading to disease.

## Open Questions

- How did the transition from Hippocratic humoral theories to more modern, evidence-based medical practices affect the integration of art and science in medical treatments?
