## Control Processes in Metacognition

The concept of control processes within the realm of metacognition is explored through an examination of observational studies on young children's problem-solving behaviors. These sources highlight that metacognitive skills, which include both monitoring and control processes, develop over time as children engage with complex tasks. Monitoring processes involve awareness of one’s cognitive activities, while control processes pertain to the regulation of these activities to achieve goals effectively. Observational analysis reveals that children exhibit an increasing ability to regulate their thinking strategies as they encounter challenges in problem-solving scenarios. However, there is a noted gap in understanding how different environments and contexts influence the development and application of these control mechanisms.

## Key Findings

- Metacognitive skills encompass both monitoring and control processes which are essential for effective cognitive regulation.
- Observational studies indicate that children's ability to regulate their thinking strategies improves with age as they tackle problem-solving tasks.
- The role of environmental factors in shaping the development of metacognitive control processes remains an underexplored area.

## Open Questions

- How do different educational and home environments influence the development of control processes in children's metacognition?
