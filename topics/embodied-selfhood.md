## Embodied Selfhood

The concept of 'embodied selfhood' emerges as a pivotal theoretical framework within the realm of dementia care and beyond. It emphasizes that an individual's sense of identity is not only shaped by cognitive processes but also deeply rooted in their physical body and its experiences. Both sources highlight how understanding embodied selfhood can significantly enhance person-centered approaches to dementia care, emphasizing the importance of acknowledging patients' bodily sensations, movements, and interactions as crucial aspects of their identity. Source 1 focuses on the social conventions that influence perceptions of embodiment, suggesting that caregiving practices should be attuned to these nuances to effectively support the holistic well-being of individuals with dementia. In contrast, Source 2 introduces a creative dimension to embodied selfhood, exploring how engaging in artistic and physical activities can not only express but also affirm an individual's sense of self within the context of their condition.

## Key Findings

- Embodied selfhood integrates both cognitive and bodily experiences into one's identity.
- Person-centered dementia care must consider the social conventions that impact perceptions of embodiment.
- Creative activities can serve as therapeutic interventions to affirm embodied selfhood.

## Open Questions

- How do cultural differences influence understanding and implementation of person-centered approaches based on embodied selfhood?
