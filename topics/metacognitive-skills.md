## Development of Metacognitive Skills

The development of metacognitive skills in young children is a critical area of study that involves understanding how these skills evolve through problem-solving activities. Observational analysis has shown that monitoring and control processes are integral to the formation of effective metacognitive strategies, indicating the importance of both reflection on one's own thought processes (monitoring) and regulation of these thoughts to achieve cognitive goals (control). Across studies, there is a consensus on the sequential nature of metacognitive skill development, moving from basic self-regulation towards more complex executive functions. However, there are tensions regarding the specific age at which certain skills become evident and how environmental factors influence this progression.

## Key Findings

- Metacognitive skills develop sequentially in young children.
- Monitoring and control processes are fundamental to metacognitive development.
- Environmental influences play a significant role in the pace of skill acquisition.

## Open Questions

- What is the impact of diverse educational environments on the timing and extent of metacognitive skill development?
