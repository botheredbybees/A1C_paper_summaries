## Age-Related Changes in Cognitive Functions

The examination of age-related changes reveals distinct trends across different cognitive functions. Source 1 focuses on the stability and variability of grapheme-to-color synesthesia, indicating that while some individuals maintain consistent associations throughout adulthood, others experience significant shifts over time. This suggests a complex interplay between genetic predisposition and environmental influences in shaping sensory experiences. In contrast, Source 2 explores the development of metacognitive skills from childhood into young adulthood, highlighting the critical role of monitoring and control processes in problem-solving tasks. These skills evolve as children grow older, becoming more sophisticated and integrated with other cognitive functions. The two sources collectively underscore the dynamic nature of cognitive abilities across the lifespan but highlight different aspects: sensory experiences versus higher-order thinking skills.

## Key Findings

- Age-related changes can be observed in both lower-level perceptual processes (like synesthesia) and higher-level cognitive processes (such as metacognition).
- Stability and variability are dual characteristics of cognitive functions across age groups, suggesting a nuanced understanding beyond simple decline or enhancement.
- Developmental trajectories of cognitive abilities vary significantly depending on the specific function being studied.

## Open Questions

- How do lower-level perceptual changes like synesthesia interact with higher-order cognitive processes such as metacognition throughout different life stages?
