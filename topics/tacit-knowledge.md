## Tacit Knowledge in Healthcare and Social Services

Across the provided sources, there is a consistent emphasis on the concept of 'tacit knowledge' as it pertains to care practices for various populations, including those with dementia and refugee youth. In 'How We Think about Dementia: Personhood, Rights, Ethics, the Arts and What They Mean for Care,' tacit knowledge is closely linked with embodied selfhood and bodily autonomy in patients, illustrating how habitual behaviors become integral to one's identity and care experience. The source highlights that these sedimented habits are often implicitly understood rather than explicitly taught or described.

In 'Transforming Dementia Care Through Theatre,' the use of theatre as an intervention method underscores the importance of tacit knowledge in fostering person-centered care, suggesting a way to engage with and understand the implicit knowledge held by both caregivers and individuals living with dementia. This approach aims at deepening mutual understanding beyond merely explicit instructions or protocols.

The third source, 'Refugee Youth and Migration: Using Arts-Informed Research to Understand Changes in Their Roles and Responsibilities,' extends this discussion of tacit knowledge into a different context—refugee youth navigating new social roles and responsibilities. Here, arts-informed research methodologies are employed as tools to uncover the symbolic expressions of these youths' experiences, indicating that such implicit understandings play a crucial role even outside traditional health care settings.

While all three sources recognize the significance of tacit knowledge in shaping caregiving practices and patient identities, they also suggest methodological challenges. The need for nuanced understanding and empathetic engagement becomes apparent when dealing with complex human interactions like those highlighted by these case studies.

## Key Findings

- Tacit knowledge plays a crucial role in healthcare and social services, influencing both the provision of care and individual identity.
- The arts provide unique methodologies to access and understand tacit knowledge within marginalized communities or those experiencing significant life changes.
- Person-centered approaches benefit from acknowledging and integrating tacit knowledge in care practices.

## Open Questions

- How can healthcare providers be trained to better recognize and utilize the implicit knowledge held by both patients and their caregivers?
