## Inclusive Arts

The synthesis of the provided sources reveals a comprehensive approach to integrating inclusivity within arts projects aimed at health and wellbeing outcomes. Source 1 emphasizes the importance of self-agency and collaborative engagement, suggesting that participants should be actively involved in decision-making processes regarding project design. This idea is reinforced by Source 4 through its mention of the social model of disability, which advocates for an inclusive environment where barriers are removed to enable everyone's full participation. Both sources highlight the need for arts projects to move beyond merely accommodating disabilities and instead focus on empowering individuals by fostering a sense of autonomy and community involvement.

Source 2 introduces the concept of 'inclusive arts,' which is further elaborated upon in Source 4 as it ties into therapeutic benefits through art-making activities designed with inclusivity at its core. This approach not only promotes social inclusion but also facilitates rehabilitation, as evidenced by Key Concept 'rehabilitation-through-art' from Source 3. By addressing the therapeutic needs of individuals alongside their social and cultural ones, inclusive arts projects can contribute significantly to holistic well-being.

Throughout these sources, there is a consistent emphasis on designing interventions that cater to diverse populations while focusing on removing societal barriers, enhancing individual self-agency, and fostering collaborative environments.

## Key Findings

- Inclusive arts projects should prioritize the social model of disability by removing barriers rather than accommodating disabilities.
- Empowering participants through self-agency and collaborative engagement is crucial for promoting holistic well-being in inclusive arts initiatives.
- Art-making activities within these projects can have therapeutic benefits, contributing to rehabilitation and overall health improvement.

## Open Questions

- How can inclusivity be effectively measured in the context of arts-based interventions?
