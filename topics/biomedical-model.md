## Biomedical Model

The biomedical model operates on a reductionist premise, focusing primarily on the biological aspects of disease and illness while marginalizing psychological, social, and environmental factors. This approach contrasts sharply with the biopsychosocial model, which integrates these additional dimensions to provide a more holistic understanding of health and wellbeing. Source 1 emphasizes that Cartesian dualism underpins the biomedical model's separation between mind and body, limiting its efficacy in addressing complex mental health issues where psychological and social determinants are significant. Meanwhile, Source 2 highlights how the biopsychosocial model offers a broader framework for comprehending stress by incorporating biological, psychological, and social elements, thereby advocating for self-care strategies that address these multifaceted aspects of human experience.

## Key Findings

- The biomedical model is reductionist, focusing narrowly on biological factors while ignoring psychological, social, and environmental determinants of health.
- Cartesian dualism informs the biomedical model's separation between mind and body, limiting its utility in treating mental health conditions.
- In contrast, the biopsychosocial model addresses health comprehensively by integrating biological, psychological, and social dimensions.

## Open Questions

- What are the limitations of applying the biomedical model to modern healthcare contexts where chronic diseases often have complex psychosocial components?
