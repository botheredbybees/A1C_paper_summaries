## Brain Aging

The studies on brain aging reveal two critical pathways of degeneration and dysfunction. The first pathway is the frontotemporal dementia (FTD) process, which involves the aggregation of abnormal proteins that impair inhibitory control and language function in the frontal lobe. This suggests a specific type of neurodegeneration linked to protein misfolding and aggregation that affects cognitive control mechanisms. In contrast, research on cortisol levels during human aging points towards another pathway involving glucocorticoids like cortisol impacting hippocampal volume, which is crucial for memory formation and retrieval. This highlights the role of chronic stress in contributing to age-related brain atrophy and cognitive decline through excessive glucocorticoid activity that disrupts normal brain function. Both studies underscore the importance of understanding different biological mechanisms underlying neurodegeneration and suggest potential targets for intervention strategies aimed at mitigating age-induced cognitive decline.

## Key Findings

- Protein aggregation is a key factor in frontotemporal dementia, affecting inhibitory control and language abilities.
- Hippocampal volume decreases with aging due to elevated cortisol levels, leading to memory impairments.
- Different neurodegenerative pathways contribute to cognitive decline in aging populations.
