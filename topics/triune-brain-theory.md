## Triune Brain Theory

The Triune Brain Theory, proposed by Paul MacLean, posits that the human brain has evolved in three stages, each adding newer layers to older ones. This model distinguishes between the reptilian complex (survival functions), the paleomammalian complex or limbic system (emotions and behavior), and the neomammalian complex (higher mental processing). Sources discuss this theory's application particularly within therapeutic contexts like psychotherapy, where gene expression alterations are suggested to be influenced by neural architecture changes. However, there is an acknowledgment of limitations in translating evolutionary theories directly into clinical applications without considering individual variability.

## Key Findings

- The Triune Brain Theory provides a framework for understanding the hierarchical organization and function of brain structures across evolution.
- In psychotherapy contexts, alterations in gene expression are proposed as consequences or indicators of neural architecture changes.
- There is an emphasis on the relevance of evolutionary theory to contemporary psychological practices.

## Open Questions

- What are the implications for individual variability when applying evolutionary theories like Triune Brain Theory in clinical practice?
