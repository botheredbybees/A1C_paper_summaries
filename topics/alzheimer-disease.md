## Alzheimer's Disease

The exploration of Alzheimer's disease (AD) reveals its multifaceted nature, with biological and therapeutic dimensions. Biologically, the accumulation of beta-amyloid plaques and tau proteins is central to AD pathology, as these impair neuronal function and communication in the brain. Treatment approaches include pharmacological interventions such as acetylcholine inhibitors aimed at mitigating cognitive decline. Additionally, immune response training vaccines represent a promising avenue for disease modification by targeting amyloid buildup. On the therapeutic front, non-pharmacological interventions like creative arts are gaining traction. These modalities enhance quality of life and cognitive function through artistic expression and cognitive rehabilitation, addressing psychological and social aspects of care that pharmacotherapy alone cannot achieve.

## Key Findings

- Accumulation of beta-amyloid plaques and tau proteins is a hallmark of Alzheimer's disease pathology.
- Pharmacological interventions like acetylcholine inhibitors are used to manage symptoms in Alzheimer's patients, although they do not address the underlying pathology.
- Creative arts therapies offer non-pharmacological benefits for cognitive rehabilitation and enhancing quality of life.

## Open Questions

- How can immune response training vaccines be optimized to effectively target and remove beta-amyloid plaques without compromising patient safety?
