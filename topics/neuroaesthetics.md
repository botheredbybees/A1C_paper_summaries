## Neuroaesthetics

Neuroaesthetics explores the neural underpinnings of human engagement with art and beauty. Sources collectively highlight that different brain regions are activated during aesthetic experiences, including the default mode network (DMN), which is associated with self-referential thought and empathy. The preservation or alteration of artistic abilities in patients suffering from various types of dementia illustrates how creativity can remain intact even when general cognitive functions deteriorate. For instance, artists diagnosed with Alzheimer's disease show preserved painting skills despite cognitive decline, suggesting a dissociation between art and cognition. Frontotemporal dementia also influences visual creativity but imposes cognitive constraints, indicating that specific brain regions associated with artistic abilities may be relatively spared during neurodegenerative processes. Key concepts such as mirror neurons play roles in empathy and the perception of art, implying that social engagement is integral to aesthetic appreciation. However, individual differences significantly influence how individuals perceive and react to art, underscoring the complexity of neurological responses to aesthetics.

## Key Findings

- Neuroaesthetics involves the default mode network (DMN) in intense aesthetic experiences.
- Artistic abilities can be preserved in neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer's disease despite cognitive decline.
- Mirror neurons are involved in empathetic responses during art perception.

## Open Questions

- How do individual differences influence neural activation patterns during aesthetic appreciation?
