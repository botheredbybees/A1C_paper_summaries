## Monitoring Processes in Metacognition

The development of metacognitive skills, as evidenced by observational analysis of young children's behavior during problem-solving activities, highlights the critical role of monitoring and control processes. Monitoring involves self-awareness and reflection on one’s own thinking and learning processes, while control pertains to strategies that learners use to manage their cognitive tasks effectively. Across studies, there is agreement on the importance of these two subprocesses in fostering metacognitive development. However, a tension arises from the differing levels at which monitoring processes operate—ranging from implicit awareness to explicit regulation—and how these vary across developmental stages and individual differences.

## Key Findings

- Monitoring and control are integral components of metacognition that develop concurrently with cognitive skills in children.
- The effectiveness of monitoring processes is contingent upon the child's ability to reflect on their thinking and learning strategies during problem-solving tasks.
- Control processes encompass a range of strategic behaviors aimed at regulating cognitive activities.

## Open Questions

- How do individual differences impact the development and efficacy of monitoring and control processes in metacognition?
