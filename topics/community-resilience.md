## Community Resilience

The integration of arts-based practices and psychosocial support in community resilience highlights a multifaceted approach to recovery from disasters. Source 1 discusses the use of Consensual Qualitative Research and the Training of Trainers model following Typhoon Yolanda, focusing on creative processes in trauma recovery. This aligns with Source 3's The Afloat Project, which employs pedagogies of visual literacy to enhance community well-being through creative recovery methods. Both sources illustrate how engaging communities in artistic activities can foster resilience by providing a platform for emotional expression and social cohesion post-disaster. In contrast, Source 2 introduces Psychological First Aid (PFA), emphasizing the necessity of timely psychosocial support to address immediate emotional and physical reactions to disasters, thus serving as an essential precursor to longer-term interventions like those described in Sources 1 and 3. The Afloat Project's emphasis on community resilience through creative recovery is further supported by Source 4’s report, reinforcing the idea that such initiatives can contribute significantly to building resilient communities capable of enduring future challenges.

## Key Findings

- Creative activities play a vital role in facilitating psychological recovery following disasters.
- Timely psychosocial support, such as Psychological First Aid (PFA), is crucial for initial disaster response.
- Community-based arts projects can enhance long-term resilience by fostering social cohesion and emotional expression.
