## Social Model Of Disability

The social model of disability is a conceptual framework that contrasts sharply with the traditional medical model. Advocates for the social model argue that it is not the individual's impairment or condition that disables them, but rather societal barriers and prejudices that prevent full participation in society. This perspective has significant implications in various fields, including arts projects, unconscious bias, and mental well-being interventions. However, critiques of the social model point to its limitations, such as its failure to address physical constraints adequately. For instance, while it highlights systemic issues affecting individuals with disabilities, it might overlook the necessity for structural adjustments like wheelchair ramps or sign language interpreters in certain settings. Another critique involves integrating Hannah Arendt's notion of power into the framework to explore further transformative possibilities that can bridge gaps left by existing critiques.

## Key Findings

- The social model of disability emphasizes societal barriers over individual impairments as disabling factors.
- Critiques suggest the social model does not adequately address physical constraints and systemic issues faced by individuals with disabilities.
- Integrating concepts from other frameworks, such as Hannah Arendt's notion of power, can potentially enhance the transformative capacity of the social model.

## Open Questions

- How might the social model be improved to better account for both societal barriers and individual physical limitations?
