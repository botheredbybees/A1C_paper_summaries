## Play Therapy

Play therapy is a therapeutic intervention specifically designed for children to facilitate the expression and resolution of emotional issues through play. It operates on the principle that children express their feelings and experiences not directly with words but through symbolic communication in the context of play. The theoretical framework of play therapy emphasizes the importance of non-verbal methods, such as toys and games, to help children articulate and process complex emotions they might struggle to communicate verbally. Sources highlight Mildred Parten's stages of play development as a foundational concept for understanding how children use various forms of play—ranging from solitary to cooperative—to convey their psychological states and needs. Both sources underscore the role of emotional intelligence in play therapy, suggesting that through play, children can develop better awareness and management of their emotions.

## Key Findings

- Play therapy uses symbolic communication and toys as a means for emotional expression.
- Mildred Parten's stages of play development provide essential insights into how different types of play correlate with psychological states in children.
- Developing emotional intelligence is a critical outcome of effective play therapy.
