## Stress Biology

The biology of stress encompasses a range of physiological responses and mechanisms that are crucial for understanding how the body reacts to both acute and chronic stress. Sources highlight the importance of the HPA (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal) axis, which is central in mediating the physiological response to stress through the release of cortisol. Additionally, Selye's concept of General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS), a theoretical model describing how organisms cope with stress over time, provides a framework for understanding the stages of stress adaptation: alarm, resistance, and exhaustion. Furthermore, neuroplasticity is identified as a key factor in the brain’s ability to adapt and change in response to stressors, indicating potential pathways for resilience and recovery. Chronic stress leads to persistent activation of these biological systems, contributing to various health issues over time. The identification of biomarkers associated with chronic stress can help in assessing individual vulnerability and predicting outcomes.

## Key Findings

- The HPA axis plays a central role in the physiological response to both acute and chronic stress through cortisol release.
- General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS) outlines stages of stress response, from alarm to resistance to exhaustion.
- Neuroplasticity is crucial for understanding how the brain adapts and changes in response to stressors.
