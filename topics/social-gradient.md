## Social Gradient

The concept of the social gradient is a cornerstone in understanding how socioeconomic status systematically influences health outcomes across populations. The idea posits that better socioeconomic conditions translate into improved health and longer life expectancy, without an upper limit where further gains cease to benefit individuals. This theory underscores the importance of addressing social determinants of health comprehensively through proportionate universalism, which advocates for policies targeting everyone but with a focus on those in greatest need. Additionally, the life course approach is highlighted as essential, emphasizing that health is shaped by experiences accumulated over a lifetime rather than solely focusing on present conditions or risk factors at specific stages.

## Key Findings

- The social gradient theory posits better socioeconomic status leads to improved health outcomes across all levels of society without an upper limit.
- Proportionate universalism and the life course approach are critical frameworks for understanding and addressing social determinants of health.

## Open Questions

- How can proportionate universalism be effectively implemented in policy-making to address health disparities arising from socioeconomic inequalities?
