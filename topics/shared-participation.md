## Shared Participation in Music Therapy

The concept of 'shared participation' in music therapy, particularly within the context of dementia care homes, is explored across sources. Source 1 introduces the idea of a 'ripple effect,' suggesting that when individuals engage in improvisational music therapy together, they experience mutual flourishing and enhanced social interaction. This collective engagement highlights the therapeutic value of shared musical experiences among participants, fostering not just individual well-being but also group cohesion and communal support.

## Key Findings

- Improvisational music therapy can lead to a 'ripple effect' where positive changes in one participant influence others.
- Mutual flourishing through shared participation in improvisational music therapy benefits both individuals and the community at large.
- Shared musical experiences promote enhanced social interaction among participants, especially within dementia care settings.

## Open Questions

- How can the therapeutic mechanisms of shared participation be further elucidated to better understand its effects on various populations?
