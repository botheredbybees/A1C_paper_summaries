## Dementia

The provided sources offer a comprehensive introduction to dementia and its various forms. Dementia is characterized by a progressive decline in cognitive functions due to brain disorders. The primary types discussed include Alzheimer's disease, vascular dementia, Lewy body dementia, and frontotemporal dementia. Each type presents distinct symptoms and physiological changes in the brain. For instance, Alzheimer's involves amyloid plaques and tau tangles interfering with nerve cell communication and causing their destruction. Vascular dementia results from damage to blood vessels leading to oxygen deprivation and subsequent neuronal death. Lewy body dementia is characterized by abnormal protein deposits that disrupt normal functioning of neurons. Frontotemporal dementia primarily affects behavior and language abilities due to deterioration in specific brain regions responsible for these functions. These conditions are marked by progressive cognitive decline, disorientation, confusion, memory loss, and changes in personality and mood. While the exact causes vary, all forms involve damage or death of nerve cells leading to irreversible impairment.

## Key Findings

- Dementia involves a progressive decline in various cognitive abilities due to brain disorders
- Alzheimer's disease is caused by amyloid plaques and tau tangles interfering with neuronal communication
- Vascular dementia results from vascular system damage leading to decreased oxygen supply and neuron death
