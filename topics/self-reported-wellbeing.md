## Self-Reported Wellbeing

The concept of self-reported wellbeing emerges as a critical indicator in the assessment of national and global happiness. The Happy Planet Index (HPI) underscores that life expectancy, along with subjective measures of individual well-being, provides a comprehensive picture beyond economic metrics alone. This index reveals discrepancies between countries where citizens report high levels of personal satisfaction but have lower environmental sustainability scores due to higher carbon footprints. Conversely, some nations score highly on ecological impact yet struggle in terms of reported happiness and longevity, hinting at the complex interplay between societal conditions and individual perceptions of wellbeing.

## Key Findings

- Self-reported well-being is a crucial component alongside life expectancy and environmental sustainability in evaluating national success beyond mere economic factors.
- High carbon footprints can correlate with lower overall self-reported wellbeing despite high levels of reported satisfaction.
- Environmental sustainability and subjective happiness are not uniformly aligned, indicating complex relationships between ecological impact and individual perceptions.

## Open Questions

- What mechanisms or policy interventions effectively balance high self-reported well-being with sustainable environmental practices?
