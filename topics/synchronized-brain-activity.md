## Synchronized Brain Activity in Autism

The examination of brain activity patterns in individuals with autism through functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) reveals distinctive features. Studies indicate that synchronized brain activity, which is crucial for normal cognitive and social functioning, shows deviations in people diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder (ASD). This discrepancy often manifests as reduced connectivity or altered synchronization between different neural networks responsible for processing social information and emotions. While some research highlights underconnectivity in the default mode network and other key areas involved in social cognition, other studies suggest overconnectivity in specific brain regions, particularly among those with high-functioning autism. These findings illustrate that synchronized brain activity patterns are disrupted in ASD, contributing to observed social dysfunctions.

## Key Findings

- Reduced or altered synchronization of neural networks is a common finding in individuals with autism spectrum disorder (ASD).
- The default mode network and other regions involved in social cognition show both underconnectivity and overconnectivity patterns among people with ASD.
- Disruptions in synchronized brain activity contribute to the observed social dysfunctions associated with autism.

## Open Questions

- What are the mechanisms underlying the variability of connectivity findings across different studies and subgroups within the spectrum?
