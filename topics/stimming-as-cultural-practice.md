## Stimming as a Cultural Practice

The concept of 'stimming as a cultural practice' is explored through the lens of neurodiversity theory. This perspective challenges traditional views by framing stimming behaviors, typically associated with autism spectrum disorder (ASD), not just as repetitive movements for sensory regulation but also as meaningful expressions within the broader context of neurological diversity. The source highlights that from an insider's viewpoint, stimming can serve communicative functions and be integral to self-expression and identity formation among neurodivergent individuals. However, power dynamics in therapy settings may sometimes undermine or pathologize these behaviors, suggesting a need for social justice frameworks that validate the cultural significance of stimming. This theoretical framework calls for an inclusive approach where clinicians respect and understand stimming as part of diverse neurological experiences rather than addressing it solely from a deficit model.

## Key Findings

- Stimming is reinterpreted within neurodiversity theory as a culturally significant practice among neurodivergent individuals, transcending mere sensory regulation.
- Traditional therapeutic approaches sometimes conflict with the cultural validation of stimming behaviors, indicating areas for improvement in power dynamics within therapy settings.
- A social justice approach supports the recognition and acceptance of stimming as part of neurological diversity.

## Open Questions

- How can clinical practices better integrate a neurodiversity-informed perspective to validate and support stimming behaviors without perpetuating stigmatization?
