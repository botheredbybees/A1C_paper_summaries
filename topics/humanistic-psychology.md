## Humanistic Psychology

Humanistic psychology is a theoretical framework that emphasizes the inherent worth and dignity of individuals. The concept of eudaimonic well-being, which centers on personal growth and self-actualization, is a cornerstone of this approach. Maslow's hierarchy of needs, prominently featured in sources one to five, illustrates the progression from basic physiological requirements through safety, love/belonging, esteem, to self-actualization. Sources two and three introduce 'self-transcendence' and Gemeinschaftsgefuhl (a sense of community) as higher stages beyond self-actualization, emphasizing interpersonal connections and shared experiences over individual achievement. The psychological well-being model discussed in source three further elucidates the importance of personal growth within humanistic theory. Misconceptions about Maslow's work are addressed in source five, clarifying that while many view his hierarchy as linear and cumulative, Maslow actually envisioned it more fluidly, with individuals often oscillating between levels.

## Key Findings

- Maslow's hierarchy of needs is central to humanistic psychology and emphasizes the progression from basic physiological needs to self-actualization.
- Beyond self-actualization lies stages such as self-transcendence and Gemeinschaftsgefuhl, highlighting interpersonal connections and shared experiences.
- The eudaimonic approach in humanistic psychology focuses on personal growth and psychological well-being.
