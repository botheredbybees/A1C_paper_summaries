## Cheerfulness

Both sources highlight the role of humor and cheerfulness as therapeutic interventions in clinical settings. Source 1 evaluates a standardized humor group for older patients with depression, noting improvements in depressive symptoms and life satisfaction. It underscores the feasibility and positive impact of integrating humor into mental health treatment plans. Meanwhile, Source 2 presents a randomized controlled trial that examines the effects of McGhee's Humor Habits Programme on palliative care patients. This intervention focuses not only on cheerfulness but also measures salivary oxytocin levels to gauge emotional well-being. Both studies emphasize the potential of humor and cheerfulness in enhancing patient outcomes, though they differ in their specific populations and measurement methods.

## Key Findings

- Humor interventions have positive effects on depressive symptoms and life satisfaction in older patients with depression.
- Cheerfulness as a therapeutic approach can be measured through changes in salivary oxytocin levels among palliative care patients.
- Standardized humor programs are feasible and acceptable within clinical settings, demonstrating potential for broader implementation.

## Open Questions

- What are the long-term effects of integrating cheerfulness and humor interventions into ongoing treatment plans?
